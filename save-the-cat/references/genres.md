# THE 10 SAVE THE CAT! GENRES

These are NOT traditional genre labels (Romantic Comedy, Epic, Biography). Those labels don't tell you anything about STORY. These 10 categories describe STORY TYPES. Every movie ever made fits into one of these.

---

## 1. MONSTER IN THE HOUSE (MITH)

**Definition:** A monster + a house + sin. The most primal story type. "Don't. Get. Eaten."

**Components:**
- **A Monster:** Supernatural or human. Must be fearsome, powerful, seemingly unstoppable
- **A House:** A CONFINED space. Beach town, spaceship, family unit, office building, summer camp. The characters cannot simply leave
- **A Sin:** Usually greed (monetary or carnal). Someone did something wrong and the monster is the avenging angel. Those who recognize the sin survive. Those who don't, die

**Rules:**
- The house must be truly confining. If characters can walk away, there's no tension (see: Arachnophobia's failure)
- The monster must have escalating, unpredictable powers
- The sin must be committed before the monster appears
- "Run and hide" is the engine

**Examples:** Jaws, Alien, The Exorcist, Fatal Attraction, Scream, Panic Room, Tremors, The Ring, Saw, A Quiet Place

**Subgenres (from Goes to the Movies):**
- Pure Monster (Alien, Jaws)
- Domestic Monster (Fatal Attraction)
- Serial Monster (Scream, Saw)
- Nihilist Monster (The Ring)
- Supra-Natural Monster (The Exorcist)

---

## 2. GOLDEN FLEECE (GF)

**Definition:** A hero goes "on the road" in search of one thing and discovers something else: HIMSELF.

**Components:**
- A road or journey (physical or metaphorical)
- Milestones along the way (people and incidents encountered)
- Internal growth driven by external events

**Rules:**
- The milestones MUST mean something to the hero. Not random episodes
- It's NOT the mileage, it's how the hero CHANGES
- The incidents themselves are almost irrelevant; what matters is their EFFECT on the hero
- Theme = internal growth. The plot is the road, the story is the change

**Includes:** Road movies AND heist movies (any quest/mission/"treasure locked in a castle")

**Examples:** Star Wars, The Wizard of Oz, Planes Trains & Automobiles, Back to the Future, Road Trip, Ocean's Eleven, The Dirty Dozen, Saving Private Ryan

---

## 3. OUT OF THE BOTTLE (OOTB)

**Definition:** "I wish I had a ___." Wish fulfillment OR comeuppance through magic (or luck).

**Two versions:**

**Wish Fulfillment:**
- Hero is a put-upon Cinderella, truly oppressed
- Magic grants the wish
- But we don't want to see anyone succeed TOO long
- Eventually hero must learn magic isn't everything, it's better to be "just like us"
- A lesson/moral is required at the end

**Comeuppance:**
- Hero is a jerk who needs a swift kick
- Must still be somewhat redeemable (needs a Save the Cat scene)
- The "curse" is really a gift that forces growth
- In the end, the hero triumphs BECAUSE of the curse

**Rules:**
- The magic doesn't have to be supernatural. Luck or coincidence works too
- The wish/curse must be directly related to the hero's flaw
- The hero must eventually CHOOSE to give up the magic

**Examples:** Liar Liar, Bruce Almighty, Freaky Friday, Blank Check, The Mask, Groundhog Day, Big, What Women Want

---

## 4. DUDE WITH A PROBLEM (DWAP)

**Definition:** An ordinary person finds themselves in extraordinary circumstances.

**Components:**
- An ORDINARY dude (just like us, relatable, no special powers)
- A BIG problem (primal, life-threatening, impossible odds)
- The hero triumphs through individuality and willingness, not special skills

**Rules:**
- The more average the guy, the bigger the challenge must be
- The badder the bad guy, the greater the heroics. Make the bad guy AS BAD AS POSSIBLE
- The hero wins by using INDIVIDUALITY to outsmart far more powerful forces
- Primal goals: save the wife, save the kids, survive

**Examples:** Die Hard, Schindler's List, The Terminator, Titanic, Breakdown, The Hunger Games

---

## 5. RITES OF PASSAGE (ROP)

**Definition:** Pain and torment from an outside force called LIFE. Growing pains, transitions, the monster you can't see or name.

**Components:**
- A life transition (puberty, mid-life, death, divorce, addiction, aging)
- An invisible "monster" (the transition itself)
- Everybody's in on the "joke" except the hero going through it
- Only experience offers the solution

**Rules:**
- The monster is unseen, vague, unnamed
- Structure follows the stages of acceptance (a la Kubler-Ross)
- The hero's journey is grudging acceptance of forces they cannot control
- Victory = the ability to ultimately smile
- The moral is always: "That's Life!"

**Examples:** 10, Ordinary People, Days of Wine and Roses, 28 Days, Napoleon Dynamite, Lost Weekend, Kramer vs. Kramer

---

## 6. BUDDY LOVE (BL)

**Definition:** ALL love stories and ALL buddy movies. They are the SAME thing.

**Components:**
- Two people (buddies, lovers, boy and dog, mentor and student)
- They hate each other at first (where would they go if they didn't?)
- Their adventure reveals they are incomplete halves of a whole
- Needing each other creates MORE conflict (who can tolerate needing anybody?)

**Structure:**
- Meet and clash
- Forced together
- Discover they need each other
- All Is Lost = separation/fight/goodbye
- Finale = surrender ego to win

**Catalyst buddy:** In many BL stories, one buddy does most of the changing (Tom Cruise in Rain Man) while the other is the CATALYST of change (Dustin Hoffman). The catalyst may change slightly but is primarily there to transform the hero.

**Examples:** Rain Man, Lethal Weapon, When Harry Met Sally, Titanic, Thelma & Louise, Finding Nemo, E.T.

---

## 7. WHYDUNIT (WD)

**Definition:** Not who did it, but WHY. An investigation into the dark side of human nature.

**Components:**
- A crime or dark secret
- A detective/surrogate who investigates on behalf of the audience
- The answer reveals something about HUMAN NATURE, not just "who did it"
- The investigation turns the x-ray back on US

**Rules:**
- The "who" is never as interesting as the "why"
- Unlike Golden Fleece, the hero doesn't necessarily change. The AUDIENCE discovers something
- Must walk the dark side. Take us to the shadowy part of the street
- The final revelation should make us ask: "Are we this evil?"

**Examples:** Chinatown, All the President's Men, JFK, Mystic River, Citizen Kane, Fargo, Blade Runner

---

## 8. FOOL TRIUMPHANT (FT)

**Definition:** The Village Idiot who is actually the wisest among us. The ultimate underdog.

**Components:**
- An underdog FOOL (seemingly inept, discounted by everyone)
- An ESTABLISHMENT to attack (White House, corporate world, high society)
- An INSIDER accomplice (who sees the truth and can't believe the Fool is getting away with it)

**Rules:**
- The Fool must be so unequipped that EVERYONE discounts them (repeatedly in setup)
- The bigger the establishment, the more satisfying the triumph
- The insider/accomplice often gets the brunt of slapstick (Herbert Lom in Pink Panther)
- We all feel like outsiders sometimes. This gives us the vicarious thrill of victory

**Examples:** Being There, Forrest Gump, Dave, The Jerk, Amadeus, Legally Blonde, The 40-Year-Old Virgin, Tootsie

---

## 9. INSTITUTIONALIZED (INST)

**Definition:** Stories about groups, institutions, and "families." The tension between individual identity and group loyalty.

**Components:**
- A GROUP (military, family, office, school, crime organization)
- A BREAKOUT character who exposes the group's goals as fraudulent
- A NEWCOMER (us, the virgin, being brought into this world)

**Rules:**
- The group dynamic is often crazy and self-destructive
- About the pros and cons of putting the group ahead of ourselves
- The newcomer character allows exposition (they can literally ask "How does that work?")
- Ultimate question: "Who's crazier, me or them?"

**Examples:** One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest, American Beauty, M*A*S*H, The Godfather, Animal House

---

## 10. SUPERHERO (SH)

**Definition:** An extraordinary person in an ordinary world. The OPPOSITE of Dude with a Problem.

**Components:**
- A special/superior being
- An ordinary world that doesn't understand them
- A NEMESIS (often someone jealous of the hero's gifts)
- The curse of being special

**Rules:**
- Must stress the PAIN of being different/special (that's how we sympathize)
- The first movie/origin story works best because it emphasizes the creation myth and sympathy
- Sequels often fail because they forget to recreate sympathy for the hero's plight
- Goes beyond capes: includes any "extraordinary person" story

**Examples:** Superman, Batman, Spider-Man, Gladiator, A Beautiful Mind, Dracula, Frankenstein, X-Men

---

## GENRE IDENTIFICATION PROCESS

When classifying a story:

1. What is the CORE dynamic? Is it a monster chasing someone? A journey? A wish? An ordinary person in danger? A life transition? A love/buddy relationship? A mystery? An underdog vs. establishment? A group? An extraordinary person?

2. Don't confuse SETTING genre (sci-fi, western, period) with STORY genre. Alien is Monster in the House, not "sci-fi." Star Wars is Golden Fleece, not "sci-fi"

3. If a movie seems to fit two categories, ask: which dynamic is the PRIMARY engine of the story? Rain Man is on the road (Golden Fleece?) but the real engine is the Tom Cruise/Dustin Hoffman relationship = Buddy Love

4. Some movies borrow from multiple genres. That's fine. But identifying the PRIMARY genre helps you know which rules to follow

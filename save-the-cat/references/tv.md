# SAVE THE CAT! WRITES FOR TV
## Jamie Nash's Adaptation of the STC Methodology for Television

Based on Save the Cat! Writes for TV by Jamie Nash and Save the Cat! Writes Horror by Jamie Nash.

---

## TV IS WONKY

The core STC beats apply to TV, but TV adds layers of complexity:

- A TV show is more than a story. It's a **STORY ENGINE** and a **STORY BRAND**
- Is the "story" an episode? A season? The entire series? (Answer: all three, nested)
- TV pilots must do double duty: tell a great standalone story AND launch a series
- The writer is king in TV. Writers are showrunners. They have more control than in film

---

## THE SHOW vs. THE PILOT

### THE SHOW (Series Concept)
Before you write a pilot, you need to answer: **What's the show?**

**Key questions:**
- What is the REPEATABLE story engine? What generates new episodes week after week?
- Who lives in this world? What's the ensemble?
- What's the "franchise" (the recurring structure that creates new stories)?
- What's the TONE? Is this a drama, comedy, dramedy?
- What network/platform is this for?

### THE PILOT (First Episode)
The pilot is a specific story told within the show's world. It must:
- Introduce the world and characters
- Tell a complete, satisfying story with beginning/middle/end
- Demonstrate the show's engine (prove this can generate infinite stories)
- Leave us wanting more (series questions that carry forward)

## THE TV BEAT SHEET

The BS2 applies to individual episodes, including the pilot. But TV has "wonky" adjustments:

### COLD OPEN
Many TV shows begin with a pre-credits sequence (the "cold open" or "teaser"). This can function as:
- A hook/grabber before the Opening Image
- The Opening Image itself
- A flash-forward or flash-back that creates mystery
- A standalone scene that establishes tone

### ACT BREAKS
Network TV has commercial breaks that function as mini-cliffhangers. Cable/streaming may not have these, but the RHYTHM of escalation still applies. Each act should end on a question, a reversal, or a surprise.

### A/B/C STORIES
TV episodes typically weave multiple storylines:
- **A Story**: The main plot of the episode
- **B Story**: The secondary plot (still carries theme, as in film)
- **C Story** (and sometimes D): Additional runner stories for supporting characters

These stories intersect and comment on each other thematically.

### PILOT-SPECIFIC BEATS

The pilot BS2 maps roughly to the film BS2, but with adjustments:

1. **Opening Image** -- Establishes the SHOW's world, not just the pilot's
2. **Theme Stated** -- Plants the series theme, not just the episode theme
3. **Set-Up** -- Must introduce the show's ensemble AND the pilot's specific situation
4. **Catalyst** -- The event that launches the PILOT story (and often the SERIES)
5. **Debate** -- "Can this work? Should I enter this new world?"
6. **Break into Two** -- The hero enters the show's world (this is often the moment that defines what the series IS)
7. **B Story** -- Secondary storyline that carries theme
8. **Fun and Games** -- The promise of the SERIES premise. This is where we see what the show will feel like week after week
9. **Midpoint** -- False victory or false defeat in the pilot story
10. **Bad Guys Close In** -- Complications escalate
11. **All Is Lost** -- The pilot's low point (may also set up season-long questions)
12. **Dark Night of the Soul** -- Brief but present
13. **Break into Three** -- The hero realizes what they need to do
14. **Finale** -- Resolves the pilot story while leaving series questions OPEN
15. **Final Image** -- Shows who the hero has become AND hints at what's to come

### KEY PILOT PRINCIPLE
The pilot must RESOLVE its episode story but OPEN its series story. The A-story wraps up. The larger questions remain.

## TV GENRES

The 10 STC genres apply to individual episodes AND to series as a whole:

- A cop show might be **Whydunit** every week (procedural)
- A family sitcom is often **Institutionalized** (group dynamics)
- A superhero show is... **Superhero**
- A survival show is **Monster in the House** or **Dude with a Problem**

But individual episodes within a series can shift genres. A cop show (Whydunit) might have a **Buddy Love** episode focused on partners' relationship, or a **Rites of Passage** episode about a character going through a personal crisis.

---

## SEASON ARC

Beyond individual episodes, a season has its own macro-beat sheet:

- **Season Opening Image**: Where is the world/hero at season start?
- **Season Catalyst**: The big event that launches the season's arc
- **Season Midpoint**: Major revelation or reversal at the season's halfway mark
- **Season All Is Lost**: The darkest moment of the season
- **Season Finale/Final Image**: Resolution and transformation

Serialized shows (Breaking Bad, Game of Thrones) lean heavily on season arcs.
Procedural shows (Law & Order, CSI) lean more on episodic arcs.
Most modern TV is somewhere in between.

## COMEDY vs. DRAMA STRUCTURES

### Half-Hour Comedy (22-30 min)
- Typically 2-3 acts
- Faster beat pacing
- Catalyst hits early (often by minute 5)
- Fun and Games is the comedy engine
- Usually A/B story structure (sometimes C)

### Hour Drama (42-60 min)
- Typically 4-6 acts (with commercial breaks) or continuous (streaming)
- Beats hit proportionally similar to film
- More room for character depth
- Usually A/B/C story structure
- Bigger set pieces and more complex plotting

### Limited Series / Miniseries
- Essentially a long movie broken into chapters
- Each episode has its own mini-arc
- Season arc IS the complete story
- BS2 applies to the full season as if it were a feature

## SAVE THE CAT! WRITES HORROR (Jamie Nash)

Horror-specific adaptations of STC:

### MONSTER IN THE HOUSE DEEP DIVE
Horror films are almost always MITH. The three components are critical:
- **The Sin**: What was done to summon the monster? This is the moral engine
- **The Monster**: Must be escalating, unknowable, relentless
- **The House**: Must be inescapable. The moment characters can leave, tension dies

### HORROR BEATS
- **Opening Image**: Often a "scare" or preview of the monster's power
- **Fun and Games**: Where the horror set pieces live (the creative kills, the escalating terror)
- **Midpoint**: Often a false victory (we think we understand the monster) or false defeat (first major death)
- **All Is Lost**: Usually the "biggest kill" or the moment where hope seems impossible
- **Finale**: Surviving = winning. The hero overcomes fear, not just the monster

### THE FEAR PRINCIPLE
The audience should be asking "What would I do?" at every beat. If the characters' choices don't resonate with how the audience would behave, the horror fails.

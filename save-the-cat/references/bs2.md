# THE BLAKE SNYDER BEAT SHEET (BS2)

## Complete 15-Beat Structure

The BS2 is based on a 110-page screenplay. Page numbers are targets, not suggestions. Hit your marks.

---

### 1. OPENING IMAGE (p. 1)
The "before" snapshot. Tone, mood, style, scope, and the starting point of the hero. This is the "before" photo in the transformation. It has a matching beat: the Final Image. These two must be OPPOSITES showing that real change has occurred.

**What it does:** Sets tone, introduces hero's world, establishes what needs to change.

---

### 2. THEME STATED (p. 5)
Someone (usually NOT the hero) makes a statement that is the movie's thematic premise. It's conversational, offhand. The hero doesn't get it yet. This is the movie's argument: "Is X true or false?" The rest of the screenplay debates this question.

**What it does:** Declares the movie's thesis. The moral argument. What the movie is ABOUT.

**Examples:**
- Blank Check: "He who has the gold makes the rules!" (Is that true?)
- Miss Congeniality: Sandra says she doesn't need to be "feminine" (But does she?)

---

### 3. SET-UP (pp. 1-10)
The first 10 pages. Make or break. Introduce or hint at EVERY character in the A story. Show the hero's world: home, work, play. Show every flaw, every behavior that needs fixing. This is the thesis world, the "before."

**Key elements:**
- Introduce all A-story characters
- The **Six Things That Need Fixing**: a laundry list of what's wrong/missing in the hero's life. These are time bombs that will be addressed later. SHOW, don't tell
- **Stasis = Death**: a sense that things can't stay like this. Change is coming whether the hero wants it or not
- The **Save the Cat moment**: hero does something that makes us LIKE them

**Structural role:** This is Act One = THESIS. The world as it is before the adventure.

---

### 4. CATALYST (p. 12)
The life-changing event. Telegram, phone call, getting fired, catching the wife in bed, a knock at the door. The set-up has told us what the world is like. Now KNOCK IT ALL DOWN. Boom!

**Rules:**
- Must happen by page 12. If your catalyst is on page 20, cut everything before it
- Often disguised as bad news, but ultimately leads to happiness
- Must be a definite, external event. Not a feeling

**Examples:**
- Romancing the Stone: The package arrives
- Rain Man: Tom Cruise learns his father died
- Legally Blonde: The fiance dumps Elle

---

### 5. DEBATE (pp. 12-25)
The hero's reaction to the catalyst. This is the last chance to say "This is crazy." It's always presented as a QUESTION: Should I go? Can I do this? Dare I try?

**What it does:** Shows how daunting the journey will be. Lets the hero (and audience) weigh the risks. Builds tension before the plunge.

**The question format is key.** Legally Blonde: "Can Elle get into Harvard Law?" The debate section answers that question.

---

### 6. BREAK INTO TWO (p. 25)
Page 25. Not 28. Not 30. Page 25.

The hero CHOOSES to enter the new world. The hero must make this decision actively. No drifting. No being tricked. The hero is proactive. That's what makes them a hero.

**Structural role:** Leaving the THESIS world and entering the ANTITHESIS. The upside-down version of the hero's world.

**Example:** Star Wars: Luke's parents are killed (event), but the DECISION to go is his. The hero cannot wake up on Han Solo's ship wondering how they got there.

---

### 7. B STORY (p. 30)
Usually the love story. ALWAYS carries the theme. A "booster rocket" that smooths over the jarring Act Two transition.

**Key functions:**
- Provides the love interest or key relationship
- Carries thematic discussion (where theme is openly debated)
- Introduces new characters (antithesis versions of Act One characters)
- Provides "cutaways" from the A story

**Example:** Legally Blonde's B Story is Elle's relationship with the manicurist. Not a traditional romance, but it's where Elle is nurtured and learns the theme.

---

### 8. FUN AND GAMES (pp. 30-55)
THE PROMISE OF THE PREMISE. This is the heart of the movie. This is where the trailer moments live. This is WHY THE AUDIENCE CAME.

**Rules:**
- Lighter in tone than other sections
- Not as concerned with forward progress of the story (stakes raise at midpoint, not here)
- This is the poster, the trailer, the hook
- When an exec asks for "more set pieces," this is where they go

**Examples:**
- Miss Congeniality: FBI agent at the beauty pageant = fun fish-out-of-water
- Stop or My Mom Will Shoot: The world's slowest chase (Mom drives)
- Die Hard: Bruce Willis outwitting terrorists
- Spider-Man: Tobey trying out his powers

---

### 9. MIDPOINT (p. 55)
The exact middle of the movie. Either a FALSE VICTORY (things seem great but aren't) or a FALSE DEFEAT (things seem terrible but will improve).

**Critical rules:**
- The stakes are RAISED at the midpoint. Fun and games are OVER
- A and B stories CROSS at the midpoint
- Has a matching beat: ALL IS LOST (p. 75). They are INVERSES
- "It's never as good as it seems at the midpoint, never as bad at All Is Lost" (or vice versa)

**The midpoint/All Is Lost axis is the backbone of your story. Nail these two and the clothesline is strung.**

---

### 10. BAD GUYS CLOSE IN (pp. 55-75)
The toughest section to write. No method gets you through it except muscle.

**What happens:**
- If midpoint was false victory: DOWNWARD path. Things get worse
- If midpoint was false defeat: UPWARD path. Things seem to improve
- External bad guys regroup and send in heavy artillery
- Internal bad guys (doubt, jealousy, dissent) tear the hero's team apart
- The hero's flaws, the ones they haven't fixed, start catching up

**Why it's hard:** You've used your best set pieces in Fun and Games. Now you need conflict that escalates from WITHIN as much as from without.

---

### 11. ALL IS LOST (p. 75)
The lowest point. The inverse of the midpoint. False defeat if midpoint was false victory. The hero is worse off than when the movie started.

**THE WHIFF OF DEATH:** Every All Is Lost moment must contain death or a symbol of death. A mentor dies (Obi-Wan in Star Wars). A flower wilts. A goldfish dies. News of an aunt's passing. It can be literal or symbolic, but it MUST be there.

**Why:** This is the "Christ on the cross" moment. The old world, old character, old way of thinking DIES here. It clears the way for synthesis.

---

### 12. DARK NIGHT OF THE SOUL (pp. 75-85)
How does the hero FEEL about hitting rock bottom? This is the "Oh Lord, why hast thou forsaken me?" moment. The darkness before the dawn.

**Rules:**
- Can last 5 seconds or 5 minutes, but it must be there
- The hero must be beaten and KNOW IT
- Only by yielding control, admitting humility, does the solution appear
- This is where the hero reaches way, deep down

---

### 13. BREAK INTO THREE (p. 85)
The "Eureka!" moment. Thanks to the B Story (love story, thematic ally), the hero finds the solution. A and B stories fuse. The hero knows what to do for both the external problem AND the internal one.

**Key:** The solution comes from the THEME. The B Story character has been discussing theme all along. Now the hero finally gets it.

---

### 14. FINALE (pp. 85-110)

Act Three. The SYNTHESIS of thesis (Act One world) and antithesis (Act Two upside-down world). A new world is born.

#### THE FIVE-POINT FINALE (from Strikes Back)

1. **Gathering the Team** -- The hero assembles allies, resources, and plans. May involve reconciliation with estranged friends or finding unexpected help

2. **Executing the Plan** -- The plan goes into action. It works... at first. Initial victories build confidence

3. **The High Tower Surprise** -- The plan FAILS or a twist occurs. The bad guys reveal a final trap, a betrayal is exposed, or the goal proves hollow. The hero must adapt

4. **Dig Deep Down** -- The hero reaches inside and finds their REAL strength, the lesson they've learned, the theme made flesh. This is where the internal change drives the external victory. No outside help. No deus ex machina. The hero alone

5. **Executing the NEW Plan** -- Armed with the real lesson, the hero improvises a new approach that synthesizes everything they've learned. Bad guys are dispatched in ascending order (lieutenants first, boss last). The new world order is established

**Other Finale rules:**
- Dispatch ALL bad guys
- Pay off the Six Things That Need Fixing
- Prove the hero has truly changed
- The hero doesn't just win, they change the WORLD

---

### 15. FINAL IMAGE (p. 110)
The "after" snapshot. The OPPOSITE of the Opening Image. Proof that change has occurred and is real.

**Examples:**
- Miss Congeniality: Opens with Sandra surrounded by boys. Closes surrounded by women. She's the Miss Congeniality winner
- The Hunger Games: Katniss starts as a survival-obsessed rule follower. Ends as a rebel

**If your Final Image doesn't mirror and invert your Opening Image, go back and check your math. Something in Act Two isn't adding up.**

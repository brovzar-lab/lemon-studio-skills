# THE IMMUTABLE LAWS OF SCREENPLAY PHYSICS

Rules discovered in the trenches of Hollywood. Violate them at your peril.

---

## THE LAWS

### 1. SAVE THE CAT
The scene where we MEET the hero and the hero does something that makes us LIKE them. It defines who they are. Without it, the audience won't root for anyone. This must happen early. It can be small (Al Pacino letting a dad go in Sea of Love) but it must be there. "Cool" is not enough. Lara Croft drives a cool car. So what? That's someone's idea of character development. It doesn't work.

### 2. THE POPE IN THE POOL
How to deliver exposition without boring the audience. Named after a scene where the Pope receives guests while swimming in the Vatican pool. You're so distracted by the visual that you don't notice you're absorbing backstory. When you have exposition to deliver, give the audience something else to look at.

### 3. DOUBLE MUMBO JUMBO
You only get ONE piece of magic per movie. If it's a genie movie, you can't ALSO have aliens. If your hero is a vampire, you can't ALSO give him telepathy (unless that's part of being a vampire). The audience will buy ONE fantastical element. Two breaks the contract.

### 4. LAYING PIPE
Too much set-up. Too much backstory. Too much explaining before the story starts. If your catalyst is on page 20, you're Laying Pipe. The set-up must be lean and the catalyst must hit by page 12. Every scene in the first 10 pages must EARN its place.

### 5. BLACK VET (aka TOO MUCH MARZIPAN)
An overly specific, "edgy" character detail that the writer thinks is interesting but actually alienates the audience. "My hero is a black veterinarian." Okay... why? If it doesn't serve the story, it's just the writer trying to seem clever. Don't be special for the sake of being special.

### 6. WATCH OUT FOR THAT GLACIER!
Danger that moves too slowly. If the threat to your hero is visible from a mile away and moves at glacial speed, there's no tension. The audience will fall asleep. Threats must be immediate, unpredictable, and escalating.

### 7. COVENANT OF THE ARC
Every character in the movie must change. Not just the hero. Every. Single. One. If a character doesn't change, they don't belong in the movie. This is the "covenant" between you and the audience: I promise that every person you meet in this story will be transformed by the events.

## THE BOARD

### What It Is
A corkboard (or wall, or artist's sketchbook) with 4 horizontal rows, each representing a quarter of the movie. You put index cards up as scene beats and arrange them until the story works.

### Layout
```
Row 1: Act One (pp. 1-25)     -- 10 cards
Row 2: Act Two A (pp. 25-55)  -- 10 cards  
Row 3: Act Two B (pp. 55-85)  -- 10 cards
Row 4: Act Three (pp. 85-110) -- 10 cards
```

### 40 Cards. No More.
That's the whole movie. Each card = one scene or sequence. If you have 50, cut. If you have 20, you're missing story.

### What Goes on a Card
- INT/EXT and location
- Basic action in simple declarative sentences ("Mary tells Joe she wants a divorce")
- Later: emotional +/- charge, conflicts, key dialogue

### Process
1. Put up your BURNING scenes first (the ones that made you want to write this)
2. Nail the HINGE points: Midpoint, Break into Two, Break into Three, All Is Lost
3. Fill in gaps
4. COLOR-CODE character storylines with different colored markers
5. Step back and look for: overloaded acts, black holes, light Act Three

### Common Board Problems

**Overloaded Act One:** 20 cards in row 1. Most of these are backstory, not scenes. Fold them into fewer, richer scenes. Character details can be SHOWN in action, not told in separate scenes.

**Black Holes:** Empty spots where you can't figure out how to connect chunks. At least you can SEE them on the Board, which is better than discovering them mid-draft.

**The Eternally Light Act Three:** Two cards: "Hero figures it out" and "The Showdown." This ALWAYS happens in early board work. Use the Five-Point Finale structure and pay off the Six Things That Need Fixing to fill it out.

**Sequences masquerading as multiple beats:** A chase is one beat even if it covers 7 locations. Write CHASE on one card.

## TROUBLESHOOTING (from "What's Wrong With This Picture?")

### Six Double-Checks for Script Repair

#### 1. THE HERO LEADS
Is your hero driving the action or being dragged through it? The hero must be PROACTIVE. They must make decisions, take risks, drive the plot forward. If they're passive observers, the audience checks out.

#### 2. MAKE THE BAD GUY BADDER
Whatever your antagonist is doing, it's not bad enough. Raise the stakes. Make the villain smarter, more ruthless, more personal. The badder the bad guy, the greater the heroics.

#### 3. TURN, TURN, TURN
Every scene must TURN. Something must change from the beginning of the scene to the end. If the emotional charge is the same at the start and finish, the scene is dead weight. Scenes go from + to - or - to +. Every time.

#### 4. THE EMOTIONAL COLOR WHEEL
Your movie shouldn't be one note. If every scene is sad, or every scene is funny, or every scene is tense, the audience goes numb. Vary the emotional tone. A drama needs moments of levity. A comedy needs moments of heart.

#### 5. "HI, HOW ARE YOU? I'M FINE"
Cut the pleasantries. Start scenes LATE and end them EARLY. Don't show characters arriving, greeting each other, and warming up. Jump in at the conflict. Jump out at the turn.

#### 6. TAKE A STEP BACK
When you're stuck, the problem is usually 10-15 pages BEFORE where you think it is. The current scene isn't working because something upstream set it up wrong. Go back further than you think you need to.

## ADDITIONAL CONCEPTS FROM STRIKES BACK

### THE SHARD OF GLASS
The wound in the hero's past that still hurts. It's the thing they haven't dealt with, the trauma they carry. It's what makes them who they are at the start and what they must finally confront by the end. Often connected to Theme Stated.

### THE DOUBLE BUMP / TRIPLE BUMP
Sometimes one catalyst isn't enough. Stubborn heroes need a DOUBLE BUMP (two catalysts) or even a TRIPLE BUMP (three) to get them moving. Each bump escalates and pushes the hero closer to the decision to act.

### THE MIDPOINT PARTY / MIDPOINT DEFEAT PATTERN
- False Victory midpoint = party/celebration/public triumph, then everything goes to hell in BGCI
- False Defeat midpoint = disaster/humiliation, then things seem to improve in BGCI (setting up the REAL fall at All Is Lost)

### STAKES ARE RAISED AT THE MIDPOINT
The midpoint isn't just a tone shift. Something HAPPENS that raises the stakes permanently. New information, a ticking clock, a public commitment, a point of no return.

### THE "SAVE THE CAT" MOMENT FOR THE BAD GUY
Great villains also get a humanizing moment. Not enough to make them sympathetic, but enough to make them three-dimensional. Even the worst bad guy should have a moment where we understand their logic, even if we reject it.

## LOGLINE CRAFT

### The Four Components of a Killer Logline

1. **IRONY** -- Must be emotionally intriguing, like an itch you have to scratch. The dramatic situation must have an ironic hook

2. **A COMPELLING MENTAL PICTURE** -- When you hear it, the whole movie blooms in your mind. You can see where it begins, where it ends, and the promise of more

3. **AUDIENCE AND COST** -- Must demarcate tone, target audience, and sense of cost so buyers know if it can make a profit

4. **A KILLER TITLE** -- Must have irony, must "say what it is," must work with the logline as a one-two punch

### Logline must also include:
- An ADJECTIVE to describe the hero
- An ADJECTIVE to describe the bad guy
- A COMPELLING PRIMAL GOAL

### The Test
Pitch it to a stranger at Starbucks. If their eyes drift, your logline has problems. If they want to know more, you've got something.

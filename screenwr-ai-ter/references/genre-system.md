# Genre System Reference
## Story Grid Genre Map + Truby Genre Forms

Genre is a contract with the audience. They show up expecting specific emotional experiences and specific narrative events. Breaking the contract loses them. Honoring it — then surprising within it — earns them.

---

## Story Grid: Two Layers of Genre

Every story has a **Content Genre** and a **External/Internal Genre** operating simultaneously. Both have obligations.

### External Genre Categories

#### Action
- **Core Value:** Life vs. Death
- **Obligatory Scenes:** Hero in mortal danger, hero fighting back, final life-or-death confrontation
- **Conventions:** Ticking clock, escalating physical jeopardy, villain with genuine lethal power, hero disadvantaged at the climax

#### Crime 
- **Core Value:** Justice vs. Injustice (or sometimes: Exposure vs. Concealment)
- **Mystery sub-genre:** Crime committed before the story begins; detective must reconstruct
- **Thriller sub-genre:** Crime being planned/executed while protagonist works to stop it
- **Obligatory Scenes:** Discovery of crime, investigation/pursuit, confrontation with criminal
- **Conventions:** Red herrings, false suspects, evidence reveals, detective's risk of becoming the accused

#### Horror
- **Core Value:** Normal vs. Abnormal (Survival vs. Damnation)
- **Sub-types:** Creature, Slasher, Dread, Supernatural, Psychological
- **Obligatory Scenes:** Monster identified, failed attempt to stop it, final confrontation — often with cost
- **Conventions:** Isolation, false safety moments, threat escalation, the monster's rules

#### Love Story / Romance
- **Core Value:** Love vs. Indifference (or Love Gained vs. Love Lost)
- **Obligatory Scenes:** Lovers meet with spark, obstacles mount between them, black moment of separation, reconciliation or loss
- **Conventions:** Attraction established early, competing love interests as optional complication, love vs. another goal, the grand gesture

#### Performance (Sports, Art, Music, etc.)
- **Core Value:** Success vs. Failure (Public performance, public judgment)
- **Obligatory Scenes:** Competition entered, adversity mounting, preparation/training, final performance
- **Conventions:** Rival who represents the protagonist's shadow, mentor figure, moment of public humiliation before triumph

#### Worldview / Coming of Age
- **Core Value:** Maturity vs. Naivety (or Cynicism vs. Hope, depending on arc direction)
- **Obligatory Scenes:** Naïve worldview confronted, loss of innocence, forced choice between worldview options
- **Conventions:** Mentor/guide figure, loss (death, betrayal, disillusionment), the moment of adult truth

#### War
- **Core Value:** Heroism vs. Cowardice (or Survival vs. Death at Scale)
- **Obligatory Scenes:** Orders received, battle/combat, death of ally, moral crisis of combat
- **Conventions:** Team ensemble, chain of command conflict, irony of war's cost vs. stated purpose

#### Society / Satire
- **Core Value:** Conformity vs. Rebellion (or Individual vs. Institution)
- **Obligatory Scenes:** Protagonist encounters the institution, attempts to work within it, is corrupted or crushed or reforms it
- **Conventions:** Absurdist logic of the system, protagonist's insider/outsider status, the cost of exposure

### Internal Genre (Character Arc Layer)

Every story also has an internal genre that describes what kind of change the protagonist undergoes. This runs simultaneously with the external genre.

| Internal Genre | Arc Type | Core Question |
|---------------|----------|--------------|
| **Morality** | Positive arc | Does the protagonist learn to put others before themselves? |
| **Worldview** | Positive/Negative arc | Does the protagonist's belief about the world change? |
| **Status** | External win/loss | Does the protagonist rise or fall in social hierarchy? |
| **Redemption** | Positive arc | Can the protagonist earn back what they've lost or done? |
| **Disillusionment** | Negative arc | Does the protagonist lose their idealism and gain truth? |
| **Education** | Positive arc | Does the protagonist learn something that fundamentally changes them? |
| **Revelation** | Flat arc | Does the protagonist reveal a truth to the world that changes others? |

## Obligatory Scenes vs. Genre Conventions

These are different. Confusing them produces scripts that feel like genre without delivering on genre.

**Obligatory Scenes:** Plot events the audience expects. Their absence produces a feeling of being cheated. You can't cut them.

**Genre Conventions:** Familiar elements the audience expects to find in the world. Can be subverted as long as the emotional function is served.

Example (Crime):
- OBLIGATORY: The detective must confront the criminal. (Cannot skip)
- CONVENTION: The criminal has a brilliant plan. (Can be subverted — a stupid criminal plan can be even more disturbing)

## Truby's Genre Networks

Truby identifies **22 master genres** that form networks of stories sharing the same deep structure. Each network has a central dramatic argument.

### Key Genre Shapes

**Ghost Story (and all hauntings):**
- The past is not past. It returns to demand accounting.
- Character is trapped between what was and what they need to become.
- Resolution = the past is either honored properly or released.

**Detective (and all investigation genres):**
- Order vs. chaos. Truth should prevail.
- The detective represents society's need to know.
- Resolution = revelation and restoration (or failure = corruption of the investigator).

**Prince and the King (succession, mentorship):**
- The young must replace the old but carry them forward.
- Central tension: readiness, legitimacy, sacrifice.
- Resolution = succession earned or refused.

**Revenge:**
- Justice denied finds its own form.
- The revenger becomes what they hate, or transcends it.
- Resolution = revenge achieved (often Pyrrhic) or abandoned (redemption).

**Love Story (the meeting of opposites):**
- Two people from different worlds are brought together and must reconcile those worlds.
- Opposition is not external — it's their fundamental difference.
- Resolution = union that transforms both, or separation that costs both.

## Genre Diagnosis Checklist

For any script, identify:
- [ ] What is the primary external genre? What are its obligatory scenes?
- [ ] Are ALL obligatory scenes present?
- [ ] Are the conventions present? Which ones are being subverted, and is the emotional function still served?
- [ ] What is the internal genre? Does the character arc match it?
- [ ] Does the external genre AND internal genre operate simultaneously at the climax? (The climax must resolve BOTH.)

**Red flags:**
- A thriller that never creates genuine fear (conventions without obligatory scenes)
- A love story where the lovers never have a real crisis that separates them
- An action movie where the hero is never in genuine danger of dying
- A character arc that resolves independently of the external plot (disconnected layers)

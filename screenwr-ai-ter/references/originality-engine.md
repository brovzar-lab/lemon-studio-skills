# Originality Engine Reference
## Taking Stories from Good to Extraordinary

Good stories follow the rules. Great stories make the rules feel like they were invented for this specific story. The difference is almost always **specificity**.

---

## The Core Principle: Specificity Over Universality

The paradox of storytelling: the more specific a story is, the more universal it feels.

**Generic:** A woman returns home after years away and confronts her difficult relationship with her mother.
**Specific:** A chef returns to Galicia to clean out her mother's fishing cottage, discovers her mother's handwritten recipe book hidden behind a wall panel, and realizes every recipe contains an ingredient she's been allergic to her whole life.

The second version is still about the mother-daughter dynamic. But now there's a world, a mystery, an image, and a question that can only belong to THIS story. The audience hasn't seen it yet.

**The specificity test:** Replace the protagonist's name and one key detail. Does the story still work? If yes, the story is too generic. The specificity isn't deep enough.

## The Uniqueness Framework

Every screenplay needs at least one answer to: "What does this story do that no other story does?"

### Five Sources of Uniqueness

**1. Setting as Meaning**
The physical world of the story actively creates meaning — not just backdrop.
- The setting has rules that pressure character psychology in specific ways
- The location embodies or opposes the theme
- Detail of the world reveals character without dialogue

*Test: Could this story happen in a different setting without losing its essential quality? If yes, the setting isn't doing enough.*

**2. Premise as Question**
The premise poses a specific question that the audience has genuinely never thought about before.
- Not: "What if someone lost their memory?" (done many ways)
- Yes: "What if the person you were before the accident was morally better than who you are now — and the person you love fell in love with who you WERE?"

*Test: Can you state the premise as a "what if" that produces genuine curiosity in someone who's never heard it?*

**3. Character Particularity**
Something about this character could not be transplanted into any other story. They carry a specific history, specific knowledge, specific contradiction that generates unique situations.
- Not: "She's a loner who learns to love."
- Yes: "She spent 12 years anonymously maintaining the Wikipedia page for her dead sister's favorite band, and now that band is playing a reunion show and she has to decide whether to appear."

*Test: Remove the protagonist's particular expertise, history, or contradiction. Does the plot collapse? If not, the particularity isn't structural — it's decoration.*

**4. Structural Surprise**
The story is structured in a way that produces the emotional experience in an unexpected sequence.
- Non-linear chronology that reveals rather than conceals
- POV shifts that recontextualize everything the audience thought they knew
- Genre blending that serves character rather than novelty

*Test: What does the structure itself communicate? A story told in reverse says something about regret. A story told in parallel says something about convergence. What does this structure say?*

**5. Thematic Specificity**
The theme is specific enough to be argued against — and the story argues it with evidence.
- Not: "Love matters."
- Yes: "The love that sustains us is often invisible to us — and only visible in its absence."

The theme should produce DISAGREEMENT. If no reasonable person would dispute it, it's not a theme. It's a sentiment.

## The Good-to-Great Elevation Moves

These are the specific interventions that take a competent, working script and make it extraordinary.

### Move 1: Specificity Injection
Identify the three most important scenes. For each, find one generic element and replace it with something that could only be true for THIS character in THIS world.

- Generic: *She looks at the photograph.* → Specific: *She checks if he blinked before she took it. He did.*
- Generic: *He pours a drink.* → Specific: *He pours from the bottle she brought him last Christmas. Still the same bottle.*

The specific detail carries ALL the emotional weight of the scene without stating it.

### Move 2: The Inevitable Surprise
Find the scene the audience knows is coming — the confrontation, the confession, the break. Now engineer it so that:
- It happens differently than expected (character logic produces an unexpected approach)
- But in retrospect, it feels like the ONLY way it could have happened

The test: Does the audience say "I didn't see that coming" AND "of course that's what happened"? That's the sweet spot.

### Move 3: The Hidden Theme Line
Every great screenplay has one line that silently states the theme without announcing it. It doesn't sound like a theme statement — it sounds like ordinary dialogue that happens to contain everything the story is about.

*Finding it:* Write the theme as a statement. Then write 10 ways a character might say something TRUE that contains this theme without using any of the same words. The best of those 10 is your hidden theme line. Plant it at the midpoint or just before the climax.

### Move 4: Earned Emotion
Emotion that is manufactured (swelling music, slow motion, a speech that explains what we should feel) turns audiences off. Emotion that is earned (prepared by earlier scenes, released at the exact right moment, expressed through behavior not words) makes them cry without knowing why.

**Earning protocol:**
1. Identify the emotional peak of your story.
2. Trace backwards: what does the audience need to have experienced to make this peak devastating?
3. Find where those experiences are in the script. If they're missing, add them.
4. Strip the manufactured emotion from the peak itself. Let the earned work do everything.

### Move 5: The Thematic Reframe
Find the scene that carries your most important thematic content. Now rewrite it from the ANTAGONIST'S emotional perspective. Not what they do — what they feel while doing it. Then restore the scene to its original POV, but let that understanding of the antagonist's inner life leak into how the protagonist perceives them.

This single move adds moral complexity to antagonists and elevates good vs. evil into good vs. understandable — which is dramatically far more powerful.

### Move 6: The Visual Promise
A great screenplay makes a visual promise in the first five pages and pays it off in the last five. Usually an image, a behavior, or a motif that appears at the beginning and returns transformed at the end.

*The image should:*
- Communicate the world and the protagonist's relationship to it
- Contain the theme in a concrete object or action
- Mean something different at the end because of everything the story has done to it

### Move 7: Compression
Most scripts are ~10% too long. Compression doesn't cut content — it cuts time. The same emotional journey in less space creates more urgency.

Compression protocol:
- Every scene: can we begin 30 seconds later and end 30 seconds earlier?
- Dialogue: every speech that's 8 lines — can it be 4?
- Transitional scenes (things that exist to move between story events): eliminate them. Cut from peak to peak.

## The Uniqueness Audit

Run this before declaring any screenplay ready:

1. **Setting test:** Does the physical world of the story actively create meaning?
2. **Premise test:** Is there a specific "what if" question non-writers find genuinely interesting?
3. **Character particularity test:** Could the protagonist be replaced by a generic hero without changing the story?
4. **Theme test:** Is the theme specific enough to be argued against?
5. **Surprise test:** Can you identify one moment that produces genuine surprise that feels inevitable in hindsight?
6. **Image test:** Is there a visual promise in the opening five pages that pays off in the last five?
7. **Specificity test:** In the three most emotional scenes, is there at least one detail that could only belong to THIS story?

If any test fails: that's where the elevation work is.

---

## Originality vs. Novelty

**Novelty:** Unusual premise, strange setting, gimmick structure. Gets attention in a pitch. Rarely earns theater tickets on its own.

**Originality:** The feeling that this story could only have been made by THIS person about THIS specific slice of human experience. Earns repeat viewings. Creates devotion.

The most original films are often not formally unusual. They're specific. They know exactly what they are and they are it completely.

The goal is not to be unlike other stories. The goal is to be so fully itself that comparison becomes irrelevant.

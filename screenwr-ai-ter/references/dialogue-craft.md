# Dialogue Craft Reference
## Subtext, Tactics, and the Russell Dialogue System

Bad dialogue says what it means. Good dialogue means what it doesn't say.

---

## The Core Problem: On-the-Nose Dialogue

On-the-nose dialogue fails because:
1. It removes subtext — the layer where the audience participates
2. It makes characters sound like they know they're in a story
3. It flattens conflict into argument rather than warfare
4. It tells the audience what to feel instead of letting them feel it

**On-the-nose:** "I'm angry at you because you never made time for me."
**Working:** "Where's the suitcase? The one I bought you for our anniversary."

The second line carries the same information but weaponizes an object. The audience does the emotional math. They feel smarter — and more invested.

---

## Tactic Catalog

Every line of dialogue is a tactic. Tactics are what characters USE to get what they want from the person they're talking to. They are not emotions — emotions are the fuel. Tactics are the weapons.

### The 24 Core Dialogue Tactics

| Tactic | What It Looks Like |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Charm** | Warmth deployed strategically to lower defenses |
| **Deflection** | Redirecting attention away from the threat |
| **Accusation** | Attacking the other person's behavior or character |
| **Moral claim** | Appealing to principle, duty, fairness, or what's right |
| **Threat** | Naming a consequence the speaker controls |
| **Humiliation** | Attacking dignity or self-image |
| **Sudden honesty** | Unexpected vulnerability or confession that disarms |
| **Feigned vulnerability** | False emotional openness as manipulation |
| **Exposure** | Revealing what the other person wants hidden |
| **Flattery** | Praise deployed to soften or obligate |
| **Bribery** | Offering something to change behavior |
| **Silence** | Refusal to engage — the absence of response as power |
| **Agreement that conceals** | Saying yes while meaning no |
| **Information withholding** | Strategic omission that the other character notices |
| **Misdirection** | Sending attention toward a false target |
| **Boundary setting** | Closing off an avenue with authority |
| **Escalation** | Raising the stakes in the middle of a verbal exchange |
| **Withdrawal** | Threatening to leave or disconnect |
| **Appeal to history** | Using shared past as leverage or weapon |
| **Reframing** | Changing how the situation is defined |
| **Projection** | Attributing one's own motives to the other person |
| **Infantilizing** | Treating the other person as less capable or rational |
| **Normalization** | Making the unacceptable seem reasonable |
| **Stalling** | Delaying response to buy time or create pressure |

## The Tactic-Shift Rule

Every dialogue volley should use a **different tactic** from the one before.

Why: If a character uses the same tactic repeatedly, every line from them sounds the same. The exchange has no internal movement. The scene stagnates.

**Flat exchange (same tactic throughout):**
```
ANNA: You need to leave. (Threat)
MARK: Make me. (Threat)
ANNA: I'll call the police. (Threat)
MARK: Go ahead. (Counter-threat)
```

**Working exchange (tactic shifts):**
```
ANNA: You need to leave. (Boundary setting)
MARK: I just want to talk. (Feigned vulnerability)
ANNA: We talked for seven years. (Appeal to history)
MARK: We never actually talked. (Reframing)
      (beat)
ANNA: The suitcase is in the hall. (Sudden honesty / Exposure)
```

The second exchange contains movement. Each line changes the field. The audience is tracking who has power.

## Subtext Mechanics

### The Iceberg Principle
The audience should see 20% of what the characters are feeling and infer the 80% below the surface. That inference IS the emotional experience.

**How to create subtext:**
1. **Give characters a concrete objective** other than the emotional one. They're arguing about who takes the dog. They're actually arguing about who gave up on the marriage.
2. **Put something at stake in the prop, location, or activity** that mirrors the emotional stakes. The suitcase. The keys. The photograph. The meal going cold.
3. **Let characters NOT say the big thing.** The unspoken thing creates pressure. The moment someone finally says it should be the scene's climax.

### Indirection
Characters approach what they really want through detours because:
- It's too vulnerable to say directly
- They don't have the right to say it yet
- They're testing the water
- They're maneuvering for position before the ask

The path TO the real conversation is where subtext lives.

### The Volcano Technique
Keep the big emotion suppressed as long as possible. Each attempt to suppress it while something forces it up = dramatic pressure. The eruption (if it comes) lands harder because of the containment.

## The Ticking Clock in Dialogue

Every scene needs urgency. Dialogue scenes use the ticking clock differently than action scenes:

**Social ticking clock:** The meal is ending. The car is arriving. The colleague can hear this.
**Psychological ticking clock:** One character is about to break composure. The other is about to walk. This cannot stay unresolved.
**Relational ticking clock:** If this conversation ends without resolution, something between them changes permanently.

The ticking clock changes how characters choose their tactics. When time is running out, desperation shifts to more aggressive or more honest choices.

## On-the-Nose Detection Protocol

Before delivering any dialogue, run these checks:

1. **Is any character explaining their own feelings directly?** ("I'm furious at you because...")
2. **Is any character explaining the plot directly?** ("As you know, Bob, the merger will...")
3. **Does any speech end with a thesis statement?** The last line of a speech should not summarize what the speech was about.
4. **Could you cut any line without changing the scene's outcome?** If yes, cut it.
5. **Are any two characters essentially agreeing with each other?** Agreement = no subtext, no tactics, no tension.

## Dialogue as Micro-Warfare

Russell's frame: every scene is a mini-war. Dialogue is the weapons system.

**Each exchange should:**
- Change who has power (even temporarily)
- Reveal character through the choice of tactic
- Press on the character's wound at least once
- Advance the beat toward or away from the beat question's answer

**A line of dialogue fails if:**
- It could be said by any character in the scene
- It says exactly what the character means without any layer beneath it
- It doesn't shift the power dynamic in any direction
- It exists to explain something to the audience rather than to affect the other character

## Vocal Fingerprinting

Every character should have a distinct voice. Not quirks or catchphrases — a distinct **psychology made audible**.

**Questions to fingerprint a voice:**
- What does this character never say directly? (Their avoidance tells you their tactic defaults)
- What's their sentence length? (Fragmented = anxious or evasive; long = controlling or seductive)
- Do they answer questions or deflect them?
- Do they use abstractions or specifics?
- Do they talk about the past, present, or future?
- Are they formal or casual? Does it shift under pressure?

**Test:** Cover the character names. Read the dialogue. Can you tell who's speaking from the voice alone? If not, voices need differentiation.

---

## Scene-Ending Line Protocol

The last line of a scene is a transition hook. It does two jobs simultaneously:
1. Closes the scene's beat (answers or deliberately leaves open the beat question)
2. Opens a new question that pulls the audience into the next beat

**What the last line should NOT do:**
- Summarize what just happened
- Tell the audience how to feel
- Resolve subtext that should stay unresolved

**Working endings:**
- A line that takes on new meaning the audience won't fully grasp until the next scene
- A silence after a question that's asked but not answered
- A decision stated with a cost visible in the delivery but not the words
- An image that the dialogue has charged with meaning

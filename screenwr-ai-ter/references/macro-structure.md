# Macro Structure Reference
## Story Grid + Weiland + Lyons — Integrated Architecture

Three frameworks describe the same underlying structure from different angles. Use all three to triangulate where the real problem is.

---

## The Weiland Structural Map

Every story beat has an arc function. Page percentages are approximate — rhythm matters more than math.

| Beat | Page % | Arc Function | What Must Happen |
|------|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Hook** | 1–5% | Lie visible in behavior | We see the Lie operating, Ghost's shadow present |
| **Inciting Event** | 10–12% | World disrupts Lie-driven status quo | Something happens that makes the ordinary world untenable |
| **First Plot Point** | 20–25% | Point of no return | Hero enters the Lie-vs-Need arena. Cannot go back. |
| **First Pinch Point** | 37–38% | Antagonistic force shows cost of the Lie | The opponent exerts pressure. Audience sees what's coming. |
| **Midpoint** | 50% | Reactive → Proactive shift | Hero makes first genuine (but inadequate) move toward the Need |
| **Second Pinch Point** | 62–63% | Partial truth challenged | Lie fights back harder. Stakes redefined. |
| **Third Act Turning Point** | 75% | Lie appears to win entirely | Ghost resurfaces. Darkest moment. No visible path forward. |
| **Climax** | 88–98% | Choice point | Hero actively accepts or rejects the Need. Must be a choice, not an event. |
| **Resolution** | 98–100% | New normal | External world reflects internal arc's truth |

## Story Grid: The Five Commandments

Every scene and every story as a whole must hit all five. They are not optional.

### 1. Inciting Incident
An event (causal or coincidental) that disrupts the protagonist's status quo. Forces them into a story problem they can't ignore.

**Causal inciting incident:** Caused by a character's action. More dramatically powerful.
**Coincidental inciting incident:** Happens to the character. Works, but requires a stronger internal dimension to compensate.

**Test:** Remove the inciting incident. Does the rest of the story still happen? If yes, it's the wrong inciting incident.

### 2. Progressive Complication / Turning Point
An event that turns the story in a new direction. Usually reveals new information or forces a choice.

**Revelatory turn:** New information that changes everything understood before.
**Active turn:** A character makes a choice that changes the stakes.

The best turning points are both simultaneously.

### 3. Crisis
The protagonist faces a dilemma with no obviously good option. Must choose between two equally weighted alternatives.

**Best Bad Choice:** Two bad options. Choosing between them reveals character.
**Irreconcilable Goods:** Two good options that cannot coexist. Sacrificing one for the other reveals character.

If the crisis has an obvious right answer, it's not a crisis — it's a problem. Rewrite until there's genuine moral ambiguity.

### 4. Climax
The protagonist makes the choice the Crisis demanded. This is the point of maximum change.

**Must be an action, not a reaction.** If the plot resolves the climax without the hero choosing, the hero is passive and the story fails.

### 5. Resolution
The fallout from the climax. Shows what the choice cost and what the new world looks like.

**Does NOT have to be happy.** Must be emotionally honest to the genre and thematic commitment.

## The Story vs. Situation Test (Lyons)

Run this test before doing any other structural work. If the script fails here, everything else is downstream.

Score Yes/No for each:
1. Does it reveal something about the human condition?
2. Does it test personal character to reveal deeper motivation?
3. Do plot twists open windows into character (not just raise stakes)?
4. Does it end in an emotionally different place than it began?
5. Is it driven by a strong moral component through the middle?

**Fail 2+:** You have a Situation, not a Story. The root problem is the moral component — go to Step 1 of Lyons BUILD mode.

**Story produces:** Interconnected scenes, active protagonist, organic escalation, earned transformation.
**Situation produces:** Episodic scenes, reactive protagonist, manufactured escalation, mechanical resolution.

## The Narrative Engine Middle (Lyons)

The middle has TWO layers operating simultaneously. Both must be present.

### Layer 1: Classic Structure (Weiland's Beats)
The beat map above. Structural skeleton.

### Layer 2: Narrative Engine (Lyons)
The behavioral-emotional mechanism that keeps the middle running.

**Offer-Refusal Loop:**
```
Immoral Effect
    → Problem/Consequence
    → Proactive Choice (hero causes their own problem)
    → Proactive Effect (makes things worse or complicates)
    → Offer to Change (implicit or explicit)
    → Refusal to Change (blind spot protects itself)
    → [Loop repeats; stakes escalate each pass]
    → Doom Moment
```

Each pass through the loop = the Pattern of Decline advancing.
Each offered change refused = one unit of de-evolution toward the Enneagram type's low point.

**Why it matters:** Without the offer-refusal loop, the hero appears to fail because of bad luck or external forces. WITH the loop, the hero is the architect of their own fall. This is the difference between tragedy and melodrama.

     ↓

     ↓

[Power shift — hero goes proactive]
     ↓

[Lie fights back — stakes redefined]
     ↓

[Hero chooses — Need accepted or rejected]
     ↓

## Midpoint Diagnosis

The midpoint is the most misunderstood beat. It is not just a major plot event. It is the moment the hero stops being reactive and starts being proactive — however inadequately, however wrongly.

**Working midpoint:** Hero makes a decision based on partial understanding of the Need. It's the right impulse for the wrong reason, or the right action executed badly. It moves the story forward but plants the seeds of the Third Act crisis.

**Broken midpoint:** A plot event happens. Stakes escalate. But the hero's internal orientation has not shifted. They're still running on pure survival instinct.

**Rewrite test:** After the midpoint, does the hero's behavior feel different from the first half? Not just more pressured — fundamentally oriented differently?

---

## The Third Act Turning Point (75%)

This beat has one job: make the Lie appear to have won completely.

The worst possible thing that can happen to THIS specific character — given their type, their Ghost, their Lie — must appear to have happened. Not generic catastrophe. Personal devastation.

**The Ghost must resurface.** The original wound that created the Lie reappears in a new form. The protagonist is back at the beginning, except worse.

**A visible path forward cannot exist.** If the audience can see how the hero will recover, the TATP isn't dark enough. Make it darker.

## Common Structural Failures

| Failure | Symptom | Root Cause |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Mushy middle** | Story meanders between Plot Point 1 and Climax | Missing offer-refusal loop; hero is reactive not causal |
| **Flat midpoint** | Nothing feels different after the 50% mark | Midpoint is a plot event, not a character orientation shift |
| **Limp third act** | Climax doesn't land emotionally | TATP wasn't dark enough; no Ghost resurfacing |
| **Unearned resolution** | Transformation feels sudden | Missing Pattern of Elevation's seven steps |
| **Passive hero** | Story happens to the protagonist | No moral component; Blind Spot doesn't cause plot |
| **Disconnected villain** | Antagonist feels generic | Not built from protagonist's de-evolution point |

# Scene Engineering Reference
## Peter Russell's BMOC Methodology (University of Story)

Russell read 3,000+ screenplays and distilled the hidden micro-structure that separates living scenes from dead ones. Every scene is a mini-war. It must **turn** and **sting**.

---

## The 15-Field Beat Card

Build this BEFORE writing a single line of dialogue.

```
BEAT CARD
─────────────────────────────────────────────
1.  BEAT QUESTION:       [Binary Yes/No. A tired viewer at 1AM can track this.]
2.  HERO OF THIS BEAT:   [Active objective + primary screen focus. NOT always the protagonist.]
3.  ANTAGONIST:          [Who/what opposes. Must have leverage.]
4.  BEGINNING (B):       [Scene opens. Status quo + immediate conflict visible in first 3 lines.]
5.  MIDDLE (M):          [Power shift #1. Something changes. Hero must adapt tactic.]
6.  OBSTACLE (O):        [Power shift #2. Stakes raised. Antagonist gains leverage or reveals weapon.]
7.  CLIMAX (C):          [Beat question answered. Win, lose, or twist. Emotionally resonant.]
8.  ANSWER PATTERN:      [YES / NO / YES-BUT / NO-AND / YES-AND — what flavor of answer?]
9.  WOUND PRESSURE:      [Which BMOC turn pressures the hero's core wound? If none, design one.]
10. TICKING CLOCK:       [What creates urgency? Social/psychological preferred over literal timer.]
11. GOOD NEWS / BAD NEWS: [List the oscillations. Aim for 3+ within the scene.]
12. STAKE ESCALATION:    [Where do stakes visibly raise? Name 2 specific moments.]
13. SURPRISE / REVERSAL: [What does the audience not see coming? Must emerge from character logic.]
14. TRANSITION HOOK:     [The question or tension that launches the next beat.]
15. SPLIT BEAT? (Y/N):   [Is BMOC interrupted? Where does the cut happen? What's concealed?]
─────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## BMOC Points — What They Must Do

### B — Beginning (~0-25%)
- Establish conflict immediately. Three lines max.
- Define hero's want and the obstacle to it.
- Set the ticking clock running.
- Do NOT start with pleasantries, small talk, or exposition unless it IS the tactics.

### M — Middle (~25-50%)
- A power shift occurs. Not just information delivered — a **choice forced**.
- Hero's first tactic fails or reveals a new complication.
- Stakes have risen from B.
- The audience recalibrates: *who's going to win this?*

### O — Obstacle (~50-75%)
- Second power shift. More severe.
- Antagonist gains leverage, or hero's vulnerability is exposed.
- The beat question feels genuinely threatened.
- Morally compromising territory — someone has to do something uncomfortable.

### C — Climax (~75-100%)
- Beat question answered. Definitively.
- Answer patterns: YES / NO / YES-BUT / NO-AND / YES-AND
- The hero has made a **choice** that drives the answer — not events arriving.
- Emotional resonance. The audience should feel it, not just register it.

## The Ten Failure Modes (Scan Every Scene)

1. **Mushy beat question** — Can't be tracked as Yes/No. Too complex or too vague.
2. **Passive antagonist** — Opposes in theory but has no leverage, no active agenda, no credible threat.
3. **No power shift** — BMOC points deliver exposition instead of forcing choices.
4. **Missing or decorative ticking clock** — Urgency is asserted but not felt.
5. **Stakes that don't escalate** — Same stakes at C as at B. Nothing has gotten worse.
6. **Split beat used as a cheat** — BMOC interrupted not for dramatic effect but to avoid writing the climax.
7. **Antagonist too weak** — No weapon. Hero faces resistance, not a genuine opponent.
8. **No tactic changes in dialogue** — Every exchange uses the same approach. Flat.
9. **Surprise from random events** — The reversal arrives from outside character logic. Unearned.
10. **Wound not pressured** — BMOC points don't touch character psychology. Scene could belong to anyone.

## Suspense Tools

Layer these onto every scene. They are not decorative — they are engineering.

### Ticking Clock
- **Literal:** Time limit imposed by plot. (Meeting in 10 minutes. Bomb. Deadline.)
- **Social:** Relationship about to be damaged irreparably if this continues.
- **Psychological:** The protagonist cannot hold their composure much longer.
- **Existential:** One more second in this situation and something about them changes forever.

Premium TV prefers social/psychological. They're harder to fake and more emotionally specific.

### Good News / Bad News Oscillations
Alternate: the hero thinks they're winning, then they're losing, then winning, then losing.
- Each oscillation should feel genuine — not manufactured.
- Minimum 3 per scene. More in long confrontational scenes.
- The final swing before C should be the most severe bad news yet.

### Stake Escalation
- **Personal stakes** must escalate (identity, relationship, survival).
- **External stakes** can escalate too, but they're secondary.
- Escalation must be visible. The audience must feel the ante going up.

## Russell's 6 Rewrite Passes

Apply in sequence. Do not combine.

### Pass 1 — Conflict Clarity
Get to the fight in the first three lines of action or dialogue. Establish want vs. opposition immediately. Cut every line that precedes the conflict if it doesn't serve it.

### Pass 2 — BMOC Engineering
Hit B at ~25%, M at ~50%, O at ~75%, C near end. These are rhythmic guidelines, not rigid math. The rhythm of turn-turn-turn is what matters. Ensure each point is a **choice**, not information.

### Pass 3 — Suspense Packing
Layer in ticking clock, good news/bad news oscillations, stake escalation. Each one should feel organic, not bolted on. If you can feel the mechanism, it's not working yet.

### Pass 4 — Surprise and Reversal
Plant a setup in B or M that pays off at C in a way the audience didn't predict but accepts as inevitable. The reversal must come from character strategy or wound, not coincidence or external events.

### Pass 5 — Character Pressure
At least one BMOC turn must press on the character's core wound (from Enneagram work or Weiland's Ghost-Lie pipeline). If wound context is unavailable, ensure at least one choice is morally compromising or identity-threatening.

### Pass 6 — Polish
Every dialogue volley uses a **different tactic**:
- Charm → Deflection → Accusation → Moral claim → Threat → Humiliation → Sudden honesty → Feigned vulnerability → Exposure → Bribery → Flattery → Silence → Agreement that hides disagreement

Cut anything that doesn't serve the beat question. If a line could be deleted without changing the outcome, delete it.

## Answer Patterns and Their Emotional Effect

| Pattern | What Happens | Emotional Effect |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **YES** | Hero gets what they want cleanly | Relief, satisfaction (use sparingly — flat if overused) |
| **NO** | Hero is denied cleanly | Frustration, tension escalation |
| **YES-BUT** | Hero wins but at a cost or with a complication | Bittersweetness, forward momentum |
| **NO-AND** | Hero is denied AND things get worse | Dread, urgency, escalation |
| **YES-AND** | Hero wins AND something unexpected opens | Surprise, new stakes, pivot |

Premium storytelling runs mostly YES-BUT and NO-AND through the middle. Pure YES or NO answers are for moments of specific dramatic effect.

## Scene Length Calibration

- Premium TV: ~2 pages per beat (28 beats/hour pilot = ~2 min per beat)
- Default: 1.5–3 pages per scene
- Over 4 pages: check if it's actually two beats. If yes, split it. Give each its own BMOC.
- Dialogue-heavy beats: can be shorter (1–2 pages)
- Action-heavy beats: can run slightly longer

---

## Split Beat Rules

A split beat is when BMOC is interrupted before C — typically at O — and resumed in a later scene.

**When to use:** For dramatic delay. The audience is left holding an unresolved beat question across a scene gap.

**Rules:**
1. The cut must happen at a genuine cliff — not a cheat.
2. The audience must know they're holding a question.
3. The aftermath scene must deliver the C that was withheld, plus whatever accumulated in the gap.

**Do NOT use as:** A way to avoid writing the climax. That's the failure mode.

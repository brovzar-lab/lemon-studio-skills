# Character Depth Reference
## Synthesizing Enneagram + Weiland + Lyons into One Pipeline

Great characters are architecturally sound AND emotionally true. This reference shows how to build both from the same foundation.

---

## The Unified Character Foundation

Every deeply felt character has three interlocking elements that come from different frameworks but describe the same wound:

| Framework | What It Calls It | What It Means |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Weiland | **Ghost** | The wound from the past that created the Lie |
| Lyons | **Moral Blind Spot** | The unconscious belief that distorts all moral decisions |
| Enneagram | **Core Fear** | The terror driving the type's survival strategy |

These are three angles on the same thing. **Build the character using all three.**

---

## Step 1: Find the Wound (Ghost + Core Fear + Blind Spot)

### Weiland's Ghost
Something happened — or conspicuously didn't happen — before the story that the character has never processed. It shaped a false belief they hold as protection.

**Discovery questions:**
- What's the first bad thing that happened to them that they never got justice for?
- What would they have had to believe about the world to survive that experience?
- What do they do now that only makes sense if you know that happened?

### Lyons' Moral Blind Spot
An unconscious core belief that twists the protagonist's moral compass. They do NOT know they have it. It is articulable in **one sentence**.

**Test:** If it takes more than one sentence, it's not specific enough. Keep cutting until you can say: *"[Character] believes that [X] — and they are wrong."*

**Two Key Questions:**
1. What would they have to believe about other people to justify acting badly?
2. What would they have to believe about themselves to justify that worldview?

### Enneagram Core Fear
The terror driving all behavior. Each type's core fear:
1. **Type 1:** Being corrupt, evil, or defective
2. **Type 2:** Being unwanted, unloved, or unneeded
3. **Type 3:** Being worthless or without value
4. **Type 4:** Being ordinary, without identity or significance
5. **Type 5:** Being useless, incapable, or incompetent
6. **Type 6:** Being without support, guidance, or security
7. **Type 7:** Being trapped in pain or deprivation
8. **Type 8:** Being controlled, harmed, or violated by others
9. **Type 9:** Being in conflict, disconnected, or lost

The Enneagram type's Core Fear usually maps directly onto the Ghost's content. A Type 6 protagonist whose father abandoned them will have a Blind Spot rooted in fear of abandonment. The frameworks confirm each other.

## Step 2: Build the Weiland Pipeline

### Ghost → Lie → Want → Need → Theme

**Lie:** The false belief the Ghost created. NOT the flaw (the flaw is behavior; the Lie is the belief producing the behavior).

**Want:** What the hero pursues externally. Serves the Lie. Drives visible plot.
> If getting the Want wouldn't threaten the Need, there's no internal tension.

**Need:** The truth that would free the character from the Lie. The direct opposite. The hero doesn't know they need this in Act 1.

**Theme:** The Need stated as an arguable universal truth.
> NOT: "Love is important." (sentiment — can't be argued against)
> YES: "The need to control what you love destroys the very thing you're trying to protect." (arguable claim)

### Pipeline Validation Test
- Ghost → created the Lie? (causally connected)
- Lie → generates the Want? (the Want is how they try to protect the Lie)
- Want → threatens the Need? (getting the Want would require ignoring or suppressing the Need)
- Need → expressed as Theme? (specific enough to be arguable)

If any link is broken, the arc will feel mechanical, not inevitable.

## Step 3: Enneagram as Arc Blueprint

The Enneagram doesn't just describe the character at the start — it maps the entire arc.

### Evolution / De-Evolution Points
Each type has two movement directions under stress:

| Type | De-Evolution (Fear) | Evolution (Safety) |
|------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 1 | → 4 (moody, self-pitying) | → 7 (joyful, spontaneous) |
| 2 | → 8 (aggressive, dominating) | → 4 (self-aware, emotional) |
| 3 | → 9 (disengaged, numbing) | → 6 (loyal, committed) |
| 4 | → 2 (smothering, needy) | → 1 (principled, disciplined) |
| 5 | → 7 (scattered, impulsive) | → 8 (decisive, commanding) |
| 6 | → 3 (image-driven, deceptive) | → 9 (relaxed, trusting) |
| 7 | → 1 (critical, perfectionistic) | → 5 (focused, perceptive) |
| 8 | → 5 (withdrawn, secretive) | → 2 (nurturing, caring) |
| 9 | → 6 (anxious, fearful) | → 3 (motivated, driven) |

**The Pattern of Decline** through the middle = protagonist sliding toward their de-evolution point under accumulating pressure from the opponent.

**The Pattern of Elevation** after the doom moment = protagonist moving toward their evolution point, which IS what the Need looks like embodied.

The evolution point is the Weiland Need made flesh as behavior.

## Step 4: Lyons Narrative Engine → Active Protagonist

This is why some characters feel passive even when they're doing things.

### Active Protagonist Loop (Story)
Blind Spot → Immoral Effect → Problem → Proactive Choice → Proactive Effect → Offer to Change → Refusal → [loop repeats, stakes escalate] → Doom Moment

### Passive Protagonist Loop (Situation)
External Problem arrives → Reactive Choice → Reactive Effect → External Problem arrives again → [loop]

**Diagnosis:** If you remove the protagonist from the middle's events and things still happen the same way, your protagonist is passive. The character must be the **cause** of their own problems through the action of their Blind Spot.

### The Offer-Refusal Loop
Every pass through the middle = an offer for the protagonist to change + a refusal.

The refusal is what keeps the story running. The protagonist cannot accept the Need yet — each time they refuse, the stakes escalate and they move further along the Pattern of Decline.

The **Doom Moment** arrives when the cost of the Blind Spot is maximum and the protagonist is at their de-evolution low point.

## Step 5: Opponent Construction (Lyons)

The main opponent must be:
- A single, personified human
- Personal to the protagonist
- Based on the protagonist's de-evolution point

**The Opponent's 8-Weapon Arsenal:**
1. Protagonist's Pattern of Decline behaviors (they mirror and amplify them)
2. De-evolution point qualities (they embody the WORST version at pathological level)
3. Core Fear/Poison (they threaten exactly what the protagonist fears most)
4. Core Desire/Distortion (they exploit what the protagonist wants most)
5. Communication Blind Spots (they use the protagonist's communication weaknesses against them)
6. Communication Distortion Filters (they exploit how the protagonist misreads information)
7. Common Conflict Pinches (they push endemic type buttons)
8. Uncommon Conflict Pinches (writer-created, specific to THIS character's history)

**Validation test:** Can you write a scene where the opponent says or does something that would devastate THIS specific protagonist but leave any other protagonist unmoved? If yes, the opposition is working.

---

## Step 6: The Pattern of Elevation (After the Doom Moment)

Seven steps from lowest point to transformation. Do not skip or compress these — rushed elevation is why third acts collapse.

1. **Doom Moment** — Rock bottom. Emotional devastation + physical isolation.
2. **First Awakening** — "I can't keep going like this." Inchoate recognition.
3. **First Step Toward Change** — Behavioral shift toward evolution point. Tentative, imperfect.
4. **Second Awakening** — "I can't do this alone." Drives them back to allies.
5. **Reconnection in a New Way** — Humility, apology. Re-entering relationships from evolution path instead of survival strategy.
6. **Final Awakening** — Allies help protagonist SEE the blind spot for the first time. Made external.
7. **Moment of Truth** — Change or lose everything. The choice that answers the thematic question.

The Weiland Climax (88-98%) maps to Steps 6-7. The Third Act Turning Point (75%) maps to Step 1.

## Secondary Characters as Arc Instruments

Secondary characters should not just add plot complexity. Their function is to amplify the protagonist's arc:

| Type | Function |
|------|----------|
| **Reflection** | Opens a window into the protagonist's moral dilemma. The gold standard. |
| **Cautionary Tale** | Represents who the protagonist will become if they don't change. |
| **Evolution Model** | Embodies the Need. Shows the protagonist what healing looks like. |
| **False Ally** | Enables the blind spot. Makes the Lie feel safe and justified. |
| **Complication** | Raises stakes, creates complications. Less depth required. |
| **Messenger** | Delivers information. No depth required. |

If a cast has no Reflection or Cautionary Tale characters, the story will feel emotionally shallow regardless of plot density.

## Character Depth Quick Diagnosis

**If the character feels flat:**
- Is there a concrete Ghost? (Not "had a hard childhood" — a specific event)
- Is the Lie articulable in one sentence?
- Is the opponent targeting the Lie specifically?
- Does the Pattern of Decline follow the character's Enneagram type?

**If the arc feels mechanical:**
- Is there a genuine Offer-Refusal loop driving the middle?
- Does the protagonist cause their own problems, or do external events just happen to them?
- Is the Doom Moment type-specific? (The lowest point should feel like THIS person's specific hell, not a generic low point.)

**If the transformation feels unearned:**
- Did the Final Awakening actually make the Blind Spot VISIBLE on screen?
- Did an ally model the evolution point in an earlier scene?
- Is there a Moment of Truth with a real cost either direction?

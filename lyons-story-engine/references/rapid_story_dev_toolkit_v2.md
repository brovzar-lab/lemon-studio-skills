## Part 1: The Seven-Step Rapid Story Development Process

This is the core methodology for developing a screenplay from the ground up. Each step feeds the next. Steps are interdependent, not a checklist.

### Step 1: Build the Enneagram Foundation of the Moral Component

The moral component is the ENGINE of any story's middle. Without it, you have a situation, not a story. Three interlocking elements:

- **Moral Blind Spot:** An unconscious core belief that twists the protagonist's moral compass and poisons all external relationships. They do NOT know they have it.
- **Immoral Effect:** The blind spot in action, how the protagonist's skewed belief produces behavior that HURTS OTHER PEOPLE on the page.
- **Dynamic Moral Tension:** The engine that keeps the story running. Every serious choice stems from the blind spot and immoral effect.

**Three discovery strategies (use in any order):**

1. Ask the two key questions: (a) "What would they have to believe about other people to justify acting badly?" (b) "What would they have to believe about themselves to justify that worldview?"
2. Look at the ending: How does the protagonist change? The lesson learned points directly back to the blind spot.
3. Use the four Enneagram building blocks: Core Fears/Poisons, Core Desires/Distortions, De-Evolution patterns, Enneagram Low Points.

### Step 2: Define the Protagonist's Enneagram Style

**Three strategies:**

1. Emotional Centers: Root emotion = fear/anxiety (Head: 5,6,7), anger/resentment (Body: 8,9,1), or shame/hostility (Heart: 2,3,4)?
2. Character Profiles: Review the nine narrative profiles (Part 2) for gut recognition.
3. Protagonist Change Triangle: Which evolution/de-evolution blueprint fits your story?

Within each center, refine: Is this character over-using, blocked, or under-using the center's function (thinking/feeling/relating)?

### Step 3: Define Evolution and De-Evolution Styles

Follow the Enneagram circuit lines. Two circuits exist:

- **Hexad Circuit (1,4,2,8,5,7):** Six styles connected by internal lines on the Enneagram diagram.
- **Triad Circuit (3,6,9):** The equilateral triangle connecting these three styles.

| Type | Center | Evo To | Evolution (Safety) | Devo To | De-Evolution (Fear) |
|------|--------|--------|-------------------|---------|---------------------|
| 1 | Body | 7 | Relax, appreciate beauty, let go of rules | 4 | Fantasy, micromanaging chaos |
| 2 | Heart | 4 | Full emotional palette, creative impulses | 8 | Controlling, forceful, dominating |
| 3 | Heart | 6 | Achieve for others' safety, not approval | 9 | Lethargy, resentment, no value |
| 4 | Heart | 1 | Focus outward, self-discipline, create something special | 2 | Needy, desperate for connection |
| 5 | Head | 8 | Visible, assertive, bring knowledge to world | 7 | Abandon thinking, numb with excess |
| 6 | Head | 9 | Peace, relaxation, enjoy work | 3 | Desperate for a win, resentful |
| 7 | Head | 5 | Think deeply, savor solitude, slow down | 1 | Critical, righteous, perfectionist |
| 8 | Body | 2 | Vulnerable, giving, helping others | 5 | Withdraw, paranoid, go it alone |
| 9 | Body | 3 | Personal presence, achievement, productive | 6 | Frozen in fear, worst-case paralysis |

### Step 4: Identify Common & Uncommon Pinches, Crunches, Blind Spots, Distortion Filters

Common = endemic to the Enneagram type. Uncommon = you invent them for this character.

**Validation for uncommon buttons:**

- Does this trait feel consistent with my character's Enneagram style?
- Does it feel right for them as an individual?
- Does it fit their general pattern of decline or elevation?

### Step 5: Define the Opposition

Main opponent should be: (1) a single person, (2) personal to the protagonist, (3) based in the Enneagram's de-evolution point.

**Opponent Triangle Components:**

- **Pattern of Decline (top left):** Protagonist's Enneagram-consistent deterioration through the middle.
- **Opponent Profile (top right):** Based on protagonist's de-evolution point. Opponent embodies the WORST qualities of that style.
- **Consequence, Character (bottom left):** Emotional state if protagonist never changes.
- **Consequence, Action (bottom right):** Plot outcome if protagonist never changes.

**The Opponent's 8-Weapon Arsenal (Enneagram resources to attack the protagonist):**

1. Pattern of Decline behaviors
2. De-Evolution point qualities
3. Core Fear/Poison
4. Core Desire/Distortion
5. Communication Blind Spots
6. Communication Distortion Filters
7. Common Conflict Pinches
8. Uncommon Conflict Pinches (writer-created)

**Two opponent subtypes to AVOID:**

- Self-Opponent: protagonist as sole enemy (too passive, kills conflict)
- Ex Machina Opponent: nature, setting, abstract forces (no personified antagonist)

### Step 6: Build the Enneagram Elements of the Story Middle

The middle has TWO layers working simultaneously:

**Layer 1: Classic Story Middle (Structural)**

1. Inciting Incident, make it PERSONAL to the blind spot, not a random event
2. First Reversal/Complication
3. Midpoint Complication, must raise BOTH community stakes AND protagonist personal stakes simultaneously
4. Second Reversal/Complication
5. Doom Moment (plot) / Low Point (character), two sides of the same coin
6. Final Battle, has a plot side AND a character side (protagonist must face blind spot)
7. Resolution, the blind spot healed in VISIBLE ACTION on the page

**Layer 2: Narrative Engine Middle (Behavioral/Emotional)**

Creates an active protagonist via the offer-refusal loop:

*Immoral Effect → Problem/Consequence → Proactive Choice → Proactive Effect → Offer to Change → Refusal to Change → [loop repeats, stakes escalate] → Doom Moment*

**Pattern of Decline (Inciting Incident → Doom Moment)**

Each pass through the offer-refusal loop moves protagonist closer to their de-evolution point. The decline follows Enneagram fear patterns.

**Pattern of Elevation (Doom Moment → Moment of Truth), 7 Steps**

1. Doom Moment, rock bottom, emotional devastation + physical isolation
2. First Awakening, "I can't keep going like this."
3. First Step Toward Change, shift to evolution path, first action
4. Second Awakening, "I can't do this alone." Drives them back to allies.
5. Reconnection in a New Way, humility, apology, rejoin from evolution path
6. Final Awakening, allies help protagonist SEE the blind spot for the first time
7. Moment of Truth, change or lose everything; heal blind spot or retreat deeper into darkness

### Step 7: Develop the Premise Line

**Four clauses capturing the full story structure:**

1. **Protagonist Clause:** Event + character (inciting incident).
2. **Team/Goal Clause:** Core relationship + tangible goal.
3. **Opposition Clause:** Opponent + adventure milestones + midpoint complication.
4. **Dénouement Clause:** Doom moment + final battle + resolution + emotional change.

## Part 1B: Reverse-Engineering an Existing Screenplay

The 7-step process runs forward (development). To ANALYZE an existing script, run it in reverse. This workflow diagnoses what's working and what isn't.

### Analysis Step 1: Is This a Story or a Situation?

**Apply the 5-point test:**

1. Does it reveal something about the human condition?
2. Does it test personal character to reveal deeper motivation?
3. Do plot twists open windows into character (not just raise stakes)?
4. Does it end in a different emotional space than it began?
5. Is it driven by a strong moral component through the middle?

*If it fails 2+ of these: the script is likely a situation. This is the root diagnosis before anything else.*

### Analysis Step 2: Identify the Protagonist's Enneagram Style

Watch for: dominant emotional center (anger/fear/shame), focus of attention, survival strategy, communication patterns. Cross-reference against the nine profiles in Part 2.

### Analysis Step 3: Diagnose the Moral Component

- Can you articulate the moral blind spot in one sentence?
- Is the immoral effect VISIBLE in behavior that hurts others, or is it merely internal angst?
- Does the protagonist make moral choices that escalate (dynamic tension), or do external events push them around (passive loop)?

*If the blind spot is vague or absent: that's why the middle is mushy.*

### Analysis Step 4: Map the Middle Structure

**Check for BOTH layers:**

- Classic layer: Can you identify inciting incident, midpoint (community + personal stakes), doom moment, final battle, resolution?
- Narrative engine layer: Is there a visible offer-refusal loop? Does the protagonist cause their own problems, or merely react to external events?
- Pattern of decline: Does the protagonist demonstrably worsen along Enneagram-consistent lines?
- Pattern of elevation: After the low point, are there awakening steps, or does the hero just magically bounce back?

### Analysis Step 5: Evaluate the Opposition

- Is the opponent a single, personified human?
- Does the opponent reflect the protagonist's de-evolution point (behavioral mirror)?
- Does the opponent attack Enneagram-specific vulnerabilities (pinches, blind spots, filters)?
- Is the opponent personal to the protagonist, or a generic "bad guy"?
- Does the opponent change at all?

### Analysis Step 6: Evaluate Supporting Characters

For each significant supporting character, classify them:

- **Messenger/Helper:** Delivers info and disappears. Fine for procedurals, but adds no depth.
- **Complication/Red-Herring:** Creates havoc, raises stakes. If overused, story becomes episodic.
- **Reflection/Cautionary Tale:** Opens a window into the protagonist's moral dilemma. This is the gold standard.

*If most characters are Messengers or Complications with zero Reflection characters: the script lacks human depth.*

### Analysis Step 7: Test the Premise Line

Can you write the four-clause premise line for this script? If you can't articulate Clause 4 (dénouement, including emotional change), the script probably doesn't have real character change.

## Part 4: Key Frameworks & Decision Tools

### The Pinch-Crunch Conflict Model

How all human (and character) conflict escalates:

1. **Start-Up (Hope):** Everyone assumes goodwill. Expectations should be shared early.
2. **Grace (Honeymoon):** No pinches yet. Good impressions all around.
3. **Irritation (Pinch):** Expectations violated. Felt as knots in stomach, jolts in head, pangs in chest. Most people say nothing.
4. **Conflict (Crunch):** After ~3+ accumulated pinches, emotions escalate dramatically. If unaddressed, becomes high-risk.
5. **Start-Over (Hostility):** Unresolved conflict damages everyone. People leave, quit, or start over.

***In stories: you WANT characters refusing to communicate, accumulating pinches, and crunching into conflict. Don't let them resolve things early.***

### Active vs. Passive Protagonist

- **Active:** ALL action generated from the moral component. Hero is unwitting architect of own fall. Loop: Blind Spot → Immoral Effect → Problem → Proactive Choice → Proactive Effect → [loop].
- **Passive:** Action generated by EXTERNAL events. Hero merely reacts. Produces situations, not stories. Loop: Problem finds them → Reactive Choice → Reactive Effect → [loop].

### Three Groupings of Supporting Characters

Every supporting character falls into one of three functional groups:

1. **Messenger/Helper:** Shows up, delivers a crucial piece of information, disappears. Common in procedurals. No subplot, no depth.
2. **Complication/Red-Herring:** Throws monkey wrenches. Raises stakes, creates havoc at the worst moment. Often exists to die horribly. Red-herring variant: not what they seem.
3. **Reflection/Cautionary Tale:** Opens a window into the protagonist's moral dilemma. Mirrors some aspect of the blind spot. The GOLD STANDARD. Cautionary variant: represents who the protagonist will become if they don't change.

*If your supporting characters don't fall into one of these groups, they're not doing anything consequential in the story.*

### Wing-Based Supporting Cast Method

Use the protagonist's Enneagram wings to generate the supporting cast:

1. Identify protagonist's core style (e.g., Nine).
2. Natural supporting cast styles = wings (Eight + One) PLUS de-evolution point (Six).
3. Each wing is a full Enneagram style, access its complete fear/safety profile.
4. When protagonist is in FEAR: supporting characters access the constricted side of their wing.
5. When protagonist is in SAFETY: supporting characters access the expansive side.
6. Layer in archetypes or cultural types AFTER the Enneagram foundation is set, never before.

### Team Stories: Four Stages of Development

1. Forming: Team comes together. Roles clarify. Honeymoon.
2. Storming: Internal conflicts. Power struggles. Leadership challenged.
3. Norming: Working agreements. Cohesion begins. Common ground.
4. Performing: Peak operation. Unified against opponent.

**Six Team Story Characteristics:**

- A clear protagonist carries the story
- Single main goal/task
- Plot is time sensitive
- Subplots serve the mainline story
- Story develops in stages
- Continuous narrative, not episodic

### Premise Line Anatomy

1. **Protagonist Clause:** Event + character (inciting incident)
2. **Team/Goal Clause:** Core relationship + tangible goal
3. **Opposition Clause:** Opponent + adventure milestones + midpoint
4. **Dénouement Clause:** Doom moment + final battle + resolution + emotional change

## Part 5: Standing Definitions

### Definition of "Moral" for Writers

*"Moral" refers to the principles, behaviors, and conduct that specify a person's sense of right and wrong in themselves, and in the world, and that define their impact in the world as a human being.*

### Supporting Character Definition

A character that is not the dramatic focus, and: may or may not have a subplot; can be an ally or opponent. Functions:

- Opens windows into protagonist's weaknesses and strengths
- Generates dramatic/comedic conflict and complications
- Challenges values and presents alternative viewpoints
- Supplies backstory, exposition, or story information unavailable to the protagonist
- Has dramatic/comedic effect on the protagonist's mainline story

### Main Opponent Definition

A supporting character who: is human (not self, nature, or abstraction); wants to stop protagonist or needs them for a mutual win on opponent's terms. Must:

- Have an Enneagram style reflecting the WORST qualities of the protagonist (de-evolution point)
- Reflect and mirror the moral component
- Actively prey on the blind spot and Enneagram vulnerabilities
- Directly contribute to the protagonist's growth or destruction

### Story vs. Situation

**A STORY:**

- Reveals something about the human condition
- Tests character to reveal deeper motivation
- Plot twists open windows into character
- Ends in a different emotional space
- Driven by strong moral component → interconnected scene writing

**A SITUATION:**

- Is a problem/puzzle with a direct solution
- Tests problem-solving skills, not character
- Plot twists ratchet mystery/stakes but don't open character windows
- Begins and ends in the same emotional space
- No/weak moral component → episodic writing

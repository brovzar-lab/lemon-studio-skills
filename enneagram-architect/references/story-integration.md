# Story Integration Guide

How to build story elements from Enneagram character design.

## The Moral Component

Every story is driven by a moral component comprising three elements:

### 1. Moral Blind Spot
The core belief the protagonist holds that twists their moral compass and poisons external relationships.

**By Type:**
| Type | Blind Spot Template |
|------|---------------------|
| One | "I must be perfect to have value" |
| Two | "I am only lovable when needed" |
| Three | "I am only valuable when successful" |
| Four | "I am only worthwhile when unique/special" |
| Five | "I must conserve resources to survive" |
| Six | "I cannot trust, so I must test" |
| Seven | "I must avoid pain at all costs" |
| Eight | "I must control or be controlled" |
| Nine | "Peace matters more than I do" |

### 2. Immoral Effect
How the blind spot hurts others through the protagonist's choices.

**By Type:**
| Type | Immoral Effect |
|------|----------------|
| One | Harsh judgment, criticism, makes others feel inadequate |
| Two | Manipulation, conditional giving, creates dependency |
| Three | Deception, abandonment of relationships for achievement |
| Four | Alienation, envy, makes others feel shallow |
| Five | Withdrawal, emotional unavailability, hoarding |
| Six | Suspicion, creates the betrayal they fear, anxiety contagion |
| Seven | Abandonment, superficiality, leaving others mid-journey |
| Eight | Domination, intimidation, crushing others' autonomy |
| Nine | Passive aggression, enabling, disappearing when needed |

### 3. Dynamic Moral Tension
The protagonist constantly faces moral choices that test them to change or disintegrate.

**Building Questions:**
- What situations force the protagonist to confront their blind spot?
- How can the plot escalate pressure on the moral wound?
- What would changing look like? What would disintegration look like?

## The Seven-Step Development Process

### Step 1: Build the Moral Component Foundation

**Key Questions:**
1. What must the protagonist believe about *others* to justify acting badly? (Worldview)
2. What must they believe about *themselves* to justify that worldview? (Blind spot)
3. What lesson do they learn at the end? (Healed blind spot)

**Building Blocks:**
- Core Fears/Poisons → Source of the fear driving the blind spot
- Core Desires/Distortions → How the desire becomes toxic
- De-Evolution patterns → The behavioral path of decline

### Step 2: Define the Protagonist's Enneagram Style

Identify for your protagonist:
- Emotional Center (Head/Body/Heart)
- Focus of Attention
- Survival Strategy
- Core Fear and Core Desire
- Core Image
- Wing lean
- Communication style (speaking patterns, blind spots, distortion filters)

### Step 3: Map Evolution and De-Evolution

**Hexad Circuit (1-4-2-8-5-7):**
| Style | De-Evolves To | Evolves To |
|-------|---------------|------------|
| One | Four (downside) | Seven (upside) |
| Four | Two (downside) | One (upside) |
| Two | Eight (downside) | Four (upside) |
| Eight | Five (downside) | Two (upside) |
| Five | Seven (downside) | Eight (upside) |
| Seven | One (downside) | Five (upside) |

**Triad Circuit (3-9-6):**
| Style | De-Evolves To | Evolves To |
|-------|---------------|------------|
| Three | Nine (downside) | Six (upside) |
| Nine | Six (downside) | Three (upside) |
| Six | Three (downside) | Nine (upside) |

**Story Application:**
- De-evolution point → Pattern of Decline template (Act 2A)
- Evolution point → Pattern of Elevation template (Act 2B/3)
- De-evolution point → Often provides ideal opponent profile

### Step 4: Design Conflict Triggers

**Common Pinches by Type:**
| Type | Predictable Triggers |
|------|---------------------|
| One | Others being sloppy, irresponsible, "wrong" |
| Two | Not being appreciated, being called manipulative |
| Three | Being ignored, failing publicly, being called a fraud |
| Four | Being told they're ordinary, being misunderstood |
| Five | Invasions of privacy, being called incompetent, emotional demands |
| Six | Unpredictability, being called paranoid, betrayal of loyalty |
| Seven | Limitations, being trapped, forced commitment |
| Eight | Being controlled, weakness exposed, injustice |
| Nine | Being forced to choose sides, conflict, being overlooked |

**Uncommon Pinches:** Story-specific buttons you design based on backstory.

**Distortion Filters:** How the protagonist mishears others—use for miscommunication that escalates conflict.

### Step 5: Design the Opposition

**Opposition Triangle:**
```
    PROTAGONIST (Type)
          ↓
    PATTERN OF DECLINE
    (De-evolution behaviors)
          ↓
    OPPONENT PROFILE
    (Based on de-evolution point)
          ↓
    CONSEQUENCES
    (Character + Action level)
```

**Best Practices:**
- Main opponent = single person (personify larger forces)
- Opponent knows protagonist's weaknesses
- Base opponent on protagonist's de-evolution point or strategic type choice
- Opponent targets blind spots, pinches, and distortion filters

### Step 6: Build the Story Middle

**Pattern of Decline (Act 2A):**
Based on de-evolution point, the protagonist's behavior deteriorates:
- Retreats into survival strategy
- Increasing stress/pressure
- Begins exhibiting de-evolution type behaviors

**Doom Moment (End of Act 2):**
| Type | Character-Side Doom |
|------|---------------------|
| One | Realizes perfection project made everything worse |
| Two | Experiences being utterly unloved despite all giving |
| Three | Faces public failure; mask stripped away |
| Four | Confronts that uniqueness led to total isolation |
| Five | Must act without sufficient knowledge; incompetence exposed |
| Six | All support systems collapse; worst fears realized |
| Seven | Completely trapped; no escape from pain |
| Eight | Powerless to protect self or others; vulnerability exposed |
| Nine | Cannot avoid conflict; forced to take a stand |

**Pattern of Elevation (Act 2B/3):**
1. Doom Moment
2. First Awakening
3. First Step Toward Change
4. Second Awakening
5. Reconnects in New Way (to human connection)
6. Final Awakening (sees blind spot clearly)
7. Moment of Truth (chooses to heal or not)

### Step 7: Develop the Premise Line

**Four Clauses:**
1. **Protagonist Clause:** An event sparks the protagonist to action
2. **Team/Goal Clause:** The protagonist joins with others toward a tangible goal
3. **Opposition Clause:** The purpose is opposed; includes midpoint complication
4. **Dénouement Clause:** From doom moment through resolution; includes emotional change

**Must capture the moral journey, not just plot.**

## Type Selection Strategy

### For Genre Fit

| Genre | Strong Type Choices | Why |
|-------|--------------------|----|
| Thriller | Six, Eight, Five | Paranoia, power, knowledge stakes |
| Romance | Two, Four, Nine | Connection, longing, merging issues |
| Comedy | Seven, Three, One | Excess, image, rigidity to subvert |
| Drama | Any type works | Match to thematic concern |
| Horror | Six, Four, Five | Fear, isolation, forbidden knowledge |
| Action | Eight, Three, One | Power, achievement, justice |

### For Thematic Resonance

| Theme | Best Protagonist Types |
|-------|----------------------|
| Trust vs. Betrayal | Six, Eight |
| Authenticity vs. Image | Three, Four |
| Connection vs. Isolation | Two, Four, Five |
| Control vs. Surrender | One, Eight, Nine |
| Pleasure vs. Pain | Seven, Four |
| Competence vs. Vulnerability | Five, Three |
| Security vs. Freedom | Six, Seven |
| Love vs. Power | Two, Eight |

### For Maximum Conflict with Specific Opponents

If you know your antagonist type, select protagonist for natural tension:
- One vs. Seven: Order vs. Chaos
- Two vs. Five: Connection vs. Distance
- Three vs. Four: Image vs. Authenticity
- Six vs. Eight: Caution vs. Power
- Nine vs. One: Acceptance vs. Perfection

## Building Ensemble Casts

### Complementary Pairing Rules

**Allies:** Should represent protagonist's evolution point or wing resources
**Opponents:** Should represent de-evolution point or strategic challenge
**Mentors:** Should embody healthy version of protagonist's type or evolution
**Love Interests:** Should create attraction through complementary needs AND conflict through type tensions

### Avoiding Redundancy

- Mix emotional centers (don't stack all Heart types)
- Mix harmonic triads (Positive Outlook, Reactive, Competency)
- Ensure someone models protagonist's evolution path
- Ensure conflict comes from type dynamics, not just plot

### Team Dynamics

If building an ensemble, assign types based on story function:
- Who provides moral compass? (likely One or Six)
- Who provides comic relief? (likely Seven or Nine)
- Who drives action? (likely Eight or Three)
- Who provides heart? (likely Two or Four)
- Who provides wisdom? (likely Five or Nine)

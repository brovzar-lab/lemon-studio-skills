# Arc Types & Structure -- Precise Mapping
## K.M. Weiland Reference

---

## The Positive Change Arc -- Full Structure Map

The hero begins believing the Lie. The story progressively dismantles it. At the climax, the hero chooses the Need.

### Act 1: The Setup (0-25%)

**Goal:** Establish the hero in their Lie-driven normal world. Make the Lie feel reasonable, even sympathetic. Make the Ghost visible in behavior without dramatizing it yet.

**The Hook (1-5%)**

First impression of the hero. Must show:
- The hero's Lie in action (not stated -- in behavior, reaction, worldview)
- The Ghost's shadow (not the Ghost itself)
- What the hero *wants* from life (the Want in its pre-plot form)
- Something that makes us invest in this person (BCI: believe, care, invest)

**The Inciting Event (10-12%)**

The plot-level disruption. Something changes in the hero's world that they can't ignore -- but they try to. The Inciting Event is the first knock on the door the hero will eventually have to answer.

This is NOT the point of no return. The hero can still, theoretically, go back to normal after the Inciting Event. They just won't.

**The First Plot Point (20-25%)**

The point of no return. The hero's Lie-driven normal world is permanently disrupted. They are now committed to Act 2 whether they want to be or not.

For the arc to function, the First Plot Point must:
- Directly engage the hero's specific Lie (not just put them in a difficult situation)
- Remove the hero's ability to keep avoiding the truth the Lie protects
- Force the hero into the Lie-vs-Need arena

Diagnostic question: If you replaced your hero with a different character who has a different Lie, would this First Plot Point still work? If yes, it's not specific enough to your hero's arc.

---

### Act 2, First Half: Reactive (25-50%)

**Goal:** Hero pursues the Want through strategies rooted in the Lie. External obstacles are, at their root, the Lie fighting back. The Need becomes visible to the audience but not yet to the hero.

**The hero's strategy in this section:**
- They're trying to solve the external problem
- Their approach is rooted in the Lie (even when they think they're being smart)
- Evidence of the Need keeps appearing, but the hero misreads it or dismisses it

**The First Pinch Point (37-38%)**

A direct reminder of the antagonistic force -- the external representation of the Lie. The hero feels the full weight of what they're up against.

The Pinch Point is not primarily a plot beat. It's a thematic beat. It shows the audience what will happen if the hero can't move past the Lie.

**What the hero must NOT do yet:** Accept the Need. They're still in avoidance mode, even if they're engaged with the problem.

---

### The Midpoint (50%)

**The pivot.** This is the most underused beat in most scripts and the most important for the arc.

Before the Midpoint: hero is **reactive** -- responding to what the plot throws at them.
After the Midpoint: hero is **proactive** -- taking initiative, however misguided.

What happens at the Midpoint:
- The hero makes their first genuine (but inadequate) move toward the Need
- They're using new tactics, but still in service of old beliefs
- They gain a partial truth that they'll have to fully accept at the Climax
- The theme becomes visible in action for the first time

**Why "inadequate"?** Because accepting the Need fully requires paying the full cost of releasing the Lie. At the Midpoint, the hero isn't ready to pay that cost. They're trying to get the benefits of the truth while keeping the Lie as a safety net.

Midpoint diagnostic questions:
- Does the hero make an active choice at the Midpoint?
- Is that choice different from the choices they were making in the first half of Act 2?
- Does the choice reflect partial movement toward the Need?
- Would the hero in Act 1 have made this choice? (If yes, nothing has changed)

---

### Act 2, Second Half: Proactive (50-75%)

**Goal:** Hero's proactive attempts based on partial truth have consequences. The Lie fights back harder. The gap between what the hero has accepted and what they still need to accept becomes clear.

**The hero's strategy in this section:**
- They're proactively pursuing what they think is right (partial truth)
- But they haven't fully released the Lie, so the partial truth creates new problems
- Relationships are strained or broken by the hero's not-fully-resolved behavior

**The Second Pinch Point (62-63%)**

The antagonistic force strikes again, harder. The hero is forced to confront the inadequacy of their current approach.

Often the Second Pinch Point involves:
- Someone the hero cares about being hurt by the hero's Lie-driven behavior
- The external antagonist gaining ground because the hero's strategy isn't working
- A direct challenge to the partial truth the hero accepted at the Midpoint

---

### The Third Act Turning Point (75%)

Also called: Dark Night of the Soul, Second Plot Point, All Is Lost moment.

**The Lie appears to have won completely.**

At 75%, the hero's entire approach -- both the Lie-driven strategies of the first half and the partial-truth strategies of the second half -- has failed. Everything is worse than it was at the start.

What happens here:
- The external situation is at its worst
- The internal arc is at its most exposed -- the Ghost resurfaces with full force
- The hero faces the fundamental question: Will you finally let go of the Lie?

The darkness at 75% must be **complete and specific**. Not generally terrible -- specifically terrible for *this hero with this Lie*. The wound from the beginning of the story must be fully reopened.

**The false resolution trap:** Many writers give the hero a small victory or insight at 75%, softening the blow. This is almost always wrong for a positive arc. The 75% turning point should be the darkest moment. The hero has nothing left. The Lie has been stripped bare. Now they either choose the Need or they don't.

Third Act Turning Point diagnostic:
- Is the hero's external situation at its absolute worst?
- Has the hero's specific Lie been fully exposed (not just their situation been worsened)?
- Does the Ghost resurface here in some form?
- Is there no obvious path forward for the hero while they're still operating on the Lie?

---

### The Climax (88-98%)

The hero chooses. Actively. Consciously.

For a positive arc:
- The hero accepts the Need -- fully, not partially
- The acceptance requires releasing the Want (or achieving it in a way that costs the Lie)
- The choice is made in action, not speech
- The choice must cost something real

The climax resolves both the external plot (the Want-driven goal) and the internal arc (the Lie vs. Need). These resolutions must happen in *proximity* -- ideally simultaneously.

How the external and internal climaxes interlock:
- Option A: Achieving the external goal requires accepting the Need (they can't win without changing)
- Option B: The hero sacrifices the Want in order to embrace the Need (they give up the goal to become who they need to be)
- Option C: The Want is achieved but is hollow without the Need (the hero gets what they wanted and realizes it doesn't help -- which forces the Need)

**The climax must be earned.** The hero's change at the Climax cannot be the first time we see them moving toward the Need. It must be the final, fullest expression of a movement that began at the Midpoint.

---

### The Resolution (98-100%)

Brief. The new normal. The world as it looks once the Lie is gone.

The Resolution should:
- Show how the hero's behavior/relationships/world have changed
- Echo the opening to show the contrast
- Not wrap everything too neatly (unless the genre demands it)

## The Negative Change Arc -- Full Structure Map

The positive arc inverted. The hero either begins with a truth and trades it for a Lie, or begins with a Lie and doubles down on it.

**Three Variations:**

### Variation 1: The Fall
- Act 1: Hero has a truth (or an idealism that functions as one)
- Acts 2-3: Hero is progressively exposed to a Lie that seems to solve their problems
- Climax: Hero chooses the Lie, loses the truth
- Examples: Macbeth, Breaking Bad (Walter White's arc)

### Variation 2: The Corruption
- Act 1: Hero has a Lie, is offered a Need (the truth)
- Acts 2-3: Hero rejects the Need progressively more completely
- Climax: Hero fully embraces the Lie, has become the thing they feared
- Examples: Michael Corleone in The Godfather trilogy

### Variation 3: The Disillusionment
- Act 1: Hero has an idealistic Lie
- Acts 2-3: Reality forces increasingly clear evidence that the Lie is false
- Climax: Hero accepts the darker truth -- sometimes the right choice, sometimes tragic
- Examples: varies widely by story

**The structural rule for negative arcs:** The same percentages apply. The difference is that the "growth" moments are actually deterioration moments, and the Pinch Points are moments where the truth is offered and rejected.

### Negative Arc Beat-by-Beat Adjustments

| Beat | Positive Arc Function | Negative Arc Function |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | Lie visible in behavior | Truth (or idealistic Lie) visible in behavior |
| First Plot Point | Forced into Lie-vs-Need arena | Forced into truth-vs-temptation arena |
| First Pinch Point | Cost of the Lie shown | Appeal of the Lie shown (or cost of holding truth) |
| Midpoint | First move toward Need | First move toward the Lie (or first rejection of truth) |
| Second Pinch Point | Partial truth challenged | Remaining truth challenged; hero pushed further toward Lie |
| Third Act Turning Point | Lie appears to win | Truth appears to win (offering the hero a last chance) |
| Climax | Hero chooses Need | Hero rejects truth, chooses Lie |
| Resolution | New normal under truth | New normal under the Lie -- the cost made visible |

## The Flat Arc -- Full Structure Map

The hero holds a truth. The world around them embodies a Lie. The story is about the hero's truth changing the world -- or failing to.

### What the flat arc hero needs:
- A clearly held truth (not a vague moral goodness -- a specific belief about the world)
- A reason that truth is tested (the world's Lie must be powerful and credible)
- A cost to holding the truth (flat arc heroes must pay for their convictions)

### The structure of a flat arc:
- Act 1: Hero and their truth established; the world's Lie established
- Act 2 First Half: Hero encounters the Lie in escalating forms; their truth is challenged
- Midpoint: Hero's first significant win *for* the truth -- inspiring a secondary character or shifting the world
- Act 2 Second Half: The Lie fights back harder; secondary characters may embrace the truth or betray it
- Third Act Turning Point: The truth appears insufficient against the Lie
- Climax: Hero holds the truth even at maximum cost; the world changes (or fails to change, in tragic flat arcs)

### Flat Arc Beat-by-Beat Adjustments

| Beat | Flat Arc Function |
|---|---|
| Hook | Hero's truth visible in behavior; world's Lie visible in environment |
| First Plot Point | Hero forced into direct conflict with the world's Lie |
| First Pinch Point | The Lie's power demonstrated; hero's truth costs them something |
| Midpoint | Hero's truth inspires someone or wins a battle (but not the war) |
| Second Pinch Point | The Lie fights back; an ally may betray the truth |
| Third Act Turning Point | The truth appears powerless against the Lie; maximum cost |
| Climax | Hero holds the truth at full cost; the world shifts (or doesn't) |
| Resolution | The world's new normal, shaped by the hero's truth |

**Secondary character arcs in flat stories:** In flat arc stories, the transformation happens in secondary characters who are changed by the hero's truth. Map those secondary characters as positive (or negative) arcs against the hero's flat arc. The hero is the catalyst; the secondaries carry the change.

## Secondary Character Arcs

Secondary characters are not decoration. In well-constructed screenplays, every significant secondary character explores the same thematic question as the protagonist -- from a different angle, with a different answer.

### The Five Secondary Arc Relationships

**1. Mirror Arc**
The secondary character has the same (or very similar) Lie as the protagonist. They face the same thematic question but may answer it differently.
- If both choose the Need: reinforcement (rare -- usually feels redundant unless one arrives first and models the choice)
- If the protagonist chooses Need but the mirror doesn't: cautionary contrast
- If the mirror chooses Need first: the mirror becomes the model for the protagonist's eventual choice
- Example: In a story where the protagonist's Lie is "I must be in control to be safe," a mirror character also believes this -- and either breaks free first (inspiring the hero) or doubles down (showing the hero what they'll become)

**2. Contrast Arc**
The secondary character has the opposite Lie or holds the truth the protagonist needs. Their arc illuminates the protagonist's by showing the other side of the same coin.
- Often the love interest or mentor figure
- Their presence makes the protagonist's Lie visible by contrast
- They may have their own arc (positive, negative, or flat) but its function in the story is to highlight the protagonist's
- Example: Protagonist believes "trust is weakness"; contrast character trusts openly and pays for it -- but also gains from it in ways the protagonist can't access

**3. Complication Arc**
The secondary character's arc creates direct obstacles for the protagonist's arc. Their pursuit of their own Want interferes with the protagonist's journey toward the Need.
- Often the B-story love interest whose own issues complicate the protagonist's
- Their arc is genuine (not just a plot device) but its structural function is to make the protagonist's arc harder
- The complication is most effective when the secondary character's Lie interacts with the protagonist's Lie in a way that makes both worse
- Example: Protagonist needs to learn vulnerability; complication character needs to learn independence. Each one's growth feels threatening to the other.

**4. Cautionary Arc (Negative Mirror)**
The secondary character shows what happens if the protagonist fails to change. They embody the Lie's endgame.
- Often an older character, a mentor gone wrong, or a rival
- They had the same opportunity the protagonist now has -- and chose the Lie
- Their presence raises the stakes: "This is who you become if you don't change"
- They may have a flat negative arc (already locked into the Lie) or a negative change arc that progresses alongside the protagonist's positive arc
- Example: The protagonist's boss who once had the same idealism but sold out decades ago

**5. Catalyst Arc**
The secondary character's arc directly triggers or accelerates the protagonist's transformation. They don't just contrast with the protagonist -- they actively force the protagonist to confront the Lie.
- Often the character who delivers the Midpoint truth or the Third Act Turning Point crisis
- May be a flat arc character whose truth challenges the protagonist's Lie
- Their key moments should align with the protagonist's major beat shifts
- Example: A child character whose innocence and honesty makes the protagonist's Lie unsustainable

### How to Use These Relationships

When diagnosing (Mode 1):
- Identify each secondary character's arc relationship to the protagonist
- Check whether the secondary arc actually serves its structural function
- Flag secondary arcs that have no clear relationship to the protagonist's thematic question

When building (Mode 2):
- After constructing the protagonist's arc, design secondary arcs that explore the theme from different angles
- Assign each secondary a relationship type and verify it creates genuine interaction (not just parallel tracks)
- Confirm at least one secondary character embodies or models the Need, and at least one embodies or escalates the Lie

When fixing (Mode 3):
- Secondary arc problems often explain protagonist arc problems. If the protagonist's midpoint doesn't land, check whether any secondary character is positioned to deliver or catalyze the partial truth
- If the climax feels unearned, check whether secondary arcs have been building toward it -- the protagonist should be surrounded by evidence of the theme playing out in other lives

### Sub-Arc Structural Timing

Secondary arcs don't need to hit every beat at the same percentages as the protagonist. But their key moments should interlock with the protagonist's arc:

- A secondary character's arc climax often occurs just BEFORE the protagonist's Third Act Turning Point or Climax -- their outcome (positive or negative) informs the protagonist's final choice
- Mirror and cautionary arcs are most effective when their resolution is visible to the protagonist before the protagonist must choose
- Catalyst arcs should deliver their most important moment at or near the protagonist's Midpoint or Third Act Turning Point
- Contrast arcs work best when their key reveal (showing the protagonist what life looks like under the truth) happens in Act 2, giving the protagonist time to metabolize it before the Climax

## Structural Diagnostic -- Complete Beat Checklist

### Act 1 (0-25%)
- [ ] Is the Lie visible in the hero's opening behavior?
- [ ] Is the Ghost present (felt, not necessarily dramatized)?
- [ ] Is the Want established?
- [ ] Is the Inciting Event (10-12%) genuinely disruptive?
- [ ] Does the First Plot Point (20-25%) directly engage the hero's specific Lie?
- [ ] Is there no going back after the First Plot Point?

### Act 2, First Half (25-50%)
- [ ] Is the hero still operating on the Lie's terms in their strategy?
- [ ] Is evidence of the Need appearing (and being dismissed)?
- [ ] Does the First Pinch Point (37-38%) show the full power of the antagonistic force?
- [ ] Does the hero remain reactive through the first half?

### Midpoint (50%)
- [ ] Does the hero shift from reactive to proactive?
- [ ] Is the hero's Midpoint choice different from their Act 1 choices?
- [ ] Is it a genuine move toward the Need, even if partial and inadequate?
- [ ] Does the theme become visible in action here?

### Act 2, Second Half (50-75%)
- [ ] Does the hero's proactive strategy create new problems?
- [ ] Does the Second Pinch Point (62-63%) challenge the partial truth?
- [ ] Is the gap between accepted truth and full truth becoming clear?

### Third Act Turning Point (75%)
- [ ] Is this the hero's darkest moment -- externally AND internally?
- [ ] Has the specific Ghost/wound resurfaced?
- [ ] Is the Lie fully exposed (not just the situation worsened)?
- [ ] Is there no obvious path forward while the Lie remains?

### Climax (88-98%)
- [ ] Does the hero make an active choice -- not just have the resolution happen to them?
- [ ] Is the choice the full acceptance of the Need?
- [ ] Does the choice cost something real?
- [ ] Do the external and internal resolutions happen in proximity?
- [ ] Is the Climax the fullest expression of a movement that began at the Midpoint?

### Resolution (98-100%)
- [ ] Does the new normal reflect the arc's truth?
- [ ] Does it echo the opening?
- [ ] Is it appropriately brief (resolution, not epilogue)?

### Secondary Arc Checklist
- [ ] Does each significant secondary character have an identifiable arc (even if small)?
- [ ] Does each secondary arc explore the same thematic question as the protagonist's?
- [ ] Is each secondary arc's relationship to the protagonist clear (mirror/contrast/complication/cautionary/catalyst)?
- [ ] Does at least one secondary character embody or model the Need?
- [ ] Does at least one secondary character embody or escalate the Lie?
- [ ] Do secondary arc climaxes happen at moments that inform the protagonist's choices?
- [ ] Are secondary arcs genuine (their own Lie/Need) rather than purely functional?
